DAY 29 - HOW TO WRITE 2 TYPES OF PROMOTIONS

I want to make this as simple as possible for you.

When I say "promotions," I mean content where you are promoting something, like a product or service.

Writing promotions is different from writing everyday content because the purpose of it changes. You don't write promotions for the purpose of likes and shares. You write promotions to generate desire, leading to them clicking on your link or call to action to take the next step.

A promotion can be anything from:

  • A social media post
  • A link as a comment to your social posts
  • A short callout in your newsletter
  • An entire newsletter with a CTA at the bottom
  • A caption on a LinkedIn, Instagram, YT community, or TikTok post
  • A series of 1-5 Instagram stories

In most cases, yours are going to be the first 4.

That means we need a short-form style of promotion and a long-form style of promotion.

For this, I use 2 frameworks:

  • PAS for short-form (problem, amplify, solution) – this is for posts and captions
  • PASTOR for long-form (problem, amplify, story, testimonial, offer, response) – this is usually for emails, sometimes captions

There are hundreds of promotion frameworks out there. They all work. I stick to these two because they're easy. Since we're building an audience, optimizing our promotions isn't a big deal. We don't have money on the line like you would have running paid ads.

With an audience, you promote as much as you'd like. 4-7 times a week usually, balanced with the amount of content you put out with a ratio of 3:1 content to promotions. So, if you stick to the frameworks above, you'll probably do just fine.

In order to write targeted promotions that our audience sees as valuable or desirable, we need to understand the 5 levels of customer awareness and write our promotions based on our audience's awareness level:

  • Unaware – unaware of their problem and how it is hurting their quality of life.
  • Problem aware – aware of their problem but don’t know how to solve it.
  • Solution aware – aware of their problem and know there is a solution, education, or knowledge to solve it.
  • Product aware – aware of their problem and know there is a streamlined path or system to solve it.
  • Most aware – they are ready to change, they just need the right “why” that changes behavior instantly. Like when you read an idea in the book and it changes your entire outlook on life.

On social media, most people live in awareness levels 1-3.

On your email list, most people live in awareness levels 3-5.

If we were to write a promotion for social media using the PAS framework, we would look for a few things:

  • Our customer avatar
  • Their pain points and the problem you solve
  • Other aspects of your offer like the unique mechanism and desired outcome for more promotional power

In a short social media promotion, you're trying to get this across as fast as possible. 280 characters or less. An example could be:

Problem: Most creatives don't make money.

Amplify: They get stuck in starving artist mode and never realize that they can learn a skill to get out.

Solution: That's why I created the 30 Day Persuasive Artist. Enroll here before midnight: [link]

Pretty simple. You should be pretty good at this after writing content and newsletters. Those skills transfer over quite well.

You can use that structure when promoting anything from your newsletter to a product to anything else you want to get someone to click on (like a YouTube video).

The difference in how many clicks and how much engagement you get, rather than just writing "I posted a new video, watch it here," is astounding.

Most people's direct promotions get 1/10 of their normal engagement rate.

When I promote my weekly newsletter, I get about 5/10 of my normal engagement rate.

You'll rarely match your actual engagement, so half of it is incredibly good.

Now, when it comes to longer promotions like emails, LinkedIn captions, or even some paid ads, I'd recommend studying the PASTOR framework in your own time.

Personally, I think it's a better decision to learn marketing with AI if you don't see yourself dedicating 3-5 years of intentional study to master it.

You'll both learn it faster with AI, and be more effective when you first start doing it.

If you're curious about how I made it, let's break it down.

Since we've been through this process multiple times, I'm going to give you the broad steps to recreate it. If you're interested, I would recommend attempting to recreate it yourself.

First, I found a PDF for the book Great Leads by M. Masterson. This is a well-known book in the copywriting space.

I dragged that into AI and asked it to create a comprehensive guide to writing good copywriting.

I saved the output as a document somewhere safe, started a new chat, used the Create Great Prompts prompt, and asked it to create a 2 phase prompt for writing promotions like emails, social media captions, and advertisements based on the word length that I give.

In phase one, I told it to interview me for my customer avatar, offer, type of media I want to create, and length of that media.

In phase two, I have apply direct response marketing (copywriting) principles based on the document we created before about how to write good copy (I pasted that in the prompt).

After the fact, I also pasted the document we saved in step one into the final prompt itself under the "Context" section. This is optional, but sometimes I notice better output when you include the exact context you want to reference in the prompt.

From there, you can iterate and refine the prompt until it is close to what you are looking for.
