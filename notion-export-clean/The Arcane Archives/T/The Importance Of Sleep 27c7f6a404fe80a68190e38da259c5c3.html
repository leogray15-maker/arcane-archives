The Importance Of Sleep

Why is sleep so important?

1) Testosterone Production

Sleep is your body’s natural TRT clinic.

Roughly ~ 50% of testosterone production happens during sleep.

If sleep was a drug you could buy over the counter, it would be banned as an illegal steroid.

That’s how powerful it is.

2) Muscle Growth

This is especially relevant for gym goers.

The majority of growth hormone release, muscle repair, and recovery happen during deep sleep.

You’re leaving gains on the table if sleep isn’t a priority.

3) Cognitive Functioning

If you care about:

  • Problem solving
  • Decision making
  • Attention and focus

You need sleep.

It’s vital for keeping memory, learning, and creativity functioning optimally.

4) Immune System

There’s nothing worse than being derailed by sickness.

The best way to prevent this?

Sleep.

It’s during sleep when immune cells and antibodies are distributed throughout the body.

5) Mental Health

Lack of sleep is highly correlated with:

  • Anxiety
  • Depression
  • Mood disorders

To function at your best, you need your mental health in check.

And your mental health is suffering if you aren’t sleeping.

6) Regulates Metabolism and Weight

The hormones that regulate hunger and fullness (grehlin and leptin) are heavily influenced by sleep.

if you want to lose weight and keep a healthy metabolism, you need to be sleeping.

7) Increases Productivity and Focus

If you want to be:

  • Alert
  • Focused
  • Present

Throughout your day, you don’t need more caffeine or more nicotine.

You need consistent sleep.

8) Longevity

Many studies show the link between sleep and lifespan.

Those who were heavily sleep deprived throughout life had:

  • Higher all-cause mortality rates
  • Lower quality of life

The choice is yours.

9) Improves Cardiovascular Health

Sleep deprivation is also linked to:

  • Increased risk of heart disease
  • Increased blood pressure
  • Increased risk of stroke

Given heart disease is the number one cause of death worldwide, it’s probably a good idea to prioritize sleep.

10) Energy

You don’t need more coffee.

You don;t need more nicotine.

You don’t need more motivation.

You need more sleep.

Stop blaming your lack of energy on external factors when it’s likely a lack of quality sleep that has you feeling tired 24/7.

Do you see now why sleep is so important?

Now let’s get into the protocols for maximising your sleep:

Every morning, view sunlight for 10+ minutes.

Why?

Getting a dose of sunlight in the morning is crucial for regulating your body's internal clock and ensuring a healthy sleep-wake cycle.

This is because sunlight activates special cells in your eyes that communicate with your brain to help regulate your sleep and set your circadian rhythm.

Aside from its role in regulating sleep, sunlight is also a major source of vitamin D, which is important for maintaining strong bones and teeth, and has been linked to a lower risk of various diseases like heart disease and cancer.

Plus it helps boost your mood, focus, and overall cognitive performance.

So, make it a habit to spend at least 10 minutes outside in the morning.

You can take a walk, stretch, or just sit and enjoy the sun.

It’s also a great way to get present, practice gratitude, and prime yourself to have a killer day.

Cut off caffeine ~ 10 hours before bed

Why?

Many of us rely on caffeine to help us power through the day, but your afternoon coffee is disrupting our sleep.

Caffeine has a long half-life - about 6 hours.

This means that if you have a coffee or pre-workout in the afternoon, the caffeine is still your system at bedtime and interfere with your sleep quality.

Say you have a 200mg energy drink or large coffee at 3pm.

At 9pm, you’ll still have 100mg circulating in your bloodstream.

And at 3am, still another 50mg.

Even if you’re someone (like myself) who can have caffeine close to bed and still fall asleep, the depth and quality of your sleep are being heavily disrupted.

Meaning you’re gonna be even more tired the next day.

Meaning you’re gonna consume even more caffeine.

And so the cycle continues.

To avoid this, it's recommended that you cut off caffeine intake at least 10 hours before bed.

So if you're aiming for an 11 pm bedtime, your last sip of coffee or pre-workout should be no later than 1 pm.

This gives your body enough time to metabolize the caffeine and ensure that it doesn't disrupt your sleep.

Cut off exercise ~ 4 hours before bed.

Why?

Intense workouts too close to bedtime keep cortisol levels high, making it difficult to fall asleep and get the rest your body needs.

Exercise also raises your body’s internal temperature, which is counterproductive to falling asleep.

To fall asleep, we need both cortisol and body temperature to drop.

So to avoid disrupting our sleep, exercise in the morning if possible.

This will not only help you avoid the cortisol and temperature spike close to bedtime but also energize you for the day ahead.

Of course morning workouts aren't feasible for everyone, but if possible try and schedule your workout in at least 4 hours before bed.

You want to be relaxed and cool to fall asleep.

Working out does the opposite.

Cut off food ~ 3 hours before bed

Why?

Eating also raises your body’s internal temperature.

To digest food, our metabolism and bodily processes have to speed up, raising bofy temp.

But as previously mentioned, our body temp needs to drop to fall into deep sleep.

To avoid this, try and cut off food at least 3 hours before bed.

This will give your body enough time to digest and cool down before you fall asleep.

Not only can this improve the quality of your sleep, but it can also improve your hormone health.

Eating too close to bedtime also keep your insulin levels elevated, which can disrupt your body's natural hormone balance.

To get into deeper sleep and keep hormonal functioning steady, aim to cut off food ~ 3 hours before bed.

Cut off work ~ 2 hours before bed.

Why?

You want to be in a relaxed mental state before bed.

Not stressing about work.

You need time to unwind.

When we work or engage in mentally demanding tasks close to bedtime, it can trigger our "fight or flight" response, releasing stress hormones like cortisol that can interfere with sleep.

Do something mindless.

  • Read a fiction book or an autobiography.
  • Brain dump journal.
  • Watch a little Netflix
  • Play some video games
  • Have sex
  • Have a casual conversation with a friend
  • Stretch
  • Do yoga
  • Meditate

The specific activity doesn’t matter.

What matters is turning your thinking, problem solving brain off.

It seems counterintuitive, but many of the highest performing athletes, entrepreneurs, and CEO’s preach by this.

One more…

1 Hour Without Blue Light

Why?

Blue light from smartphones, laptops, and televisions, can significantly mess up our ability to fall asleep and stay asleep.

Here are a few reasons why…

  • Blue light suppresses melatonin production:Melatonin is a hormone that regulates our sleep-wake cycle.

    Exposure to blue light can suppress the production of melatonin, making it harder to fall asleep and stay asleep throughout the night.

  • Blue light disrupts our circadian rhythm:

    Our bodies have an internal "clock" called the circadian rhythm that regulates our sleep-wake cycle.Exposure to blue light at night can disrupt our circadian rhythm, making it harder to fall asleep and leading to poor sleep quality.

  • Blue light can impact our health:Chronic exposure to blue light at night has been linked to a range of health issues, including obesity, diabetes, and depression.

By cutting off blue light exposure at least 1 hour before bed, we give our bodies time to adjust to the natural decrease in light that occurs as we approach bedtime.

This can help regulate our circadian rhythm, improve melatonin production, and promote better sleep quality overall.

I like to turn my phone on airplane mode an hour before bed.
