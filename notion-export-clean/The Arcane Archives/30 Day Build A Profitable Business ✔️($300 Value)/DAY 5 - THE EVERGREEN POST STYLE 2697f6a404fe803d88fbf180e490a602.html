That's why we're spending so much time on it.

Your bio, profile picture, website, and everything else is not a habit, it's a one time thing with minor revisions down the road.

95% of your time building your brand will be spent writing content.

Today, we will be writing listicles.

A listicle is an evergreen content style with a very simple structure: hook > bullet list > conclusion.

These practically write themselves. You think of a hook like "how to X" or "the best skills to learn are" and ideas start flowing naturally.

Why do listicles work?

  • They capture attention fast
  • They guide the reader down the post
  • The reader has multiple chances to engage, meaning they can agree or disagree with any point in the list which leads to a like, comment, or share. If they agree with multiple points, they are much more likely to do so.
  • You can use most points from your list as the starting point of another post

Listicles have been around forever.

"10 ways to this" and "how to that in 5 steps" are headlines you've seen across Buzzfeed, Medium, YouTube, and any other social platform.

Why? Because they work.

Listicles are a post style you can resort to when you have no idea what to say. As a bonus, they are great at generating ideas for other posts. Starting a post writing session with a listicle is a great way to get ideas flowing.

We're going to write 3 types of listicles.

One "how to"

One "reasons why"

One "pain points"

Write A "How To" Listicle

Notice how I didn't start with "how to" for the hook.

You can easily start with "how to make the most of your 20s," but if you are consistently writing this type of content, it will look like you're always repeating yourself by starting with "how to."

Instead, I thought of a "how to" topic, like how to turn your life around, even though there isn't anything in the post that directly states that. It's the idea.

Here's what you do:

  • Think of a broad "how to" idea related to your topics
  • List out 3-7 bullet points that are at least slightly unique to you
  • End with a unique perspective, usually in the form of a "why" statement (answer "why should I do this?" with 1-2 sentences)
  • Think of a hook that flows nicely. You can either write this first or after you brainstorm the post.

Pro-tip: it helps to keep your bullet points short enough so that they don't break onto another line. When people are viewing it on mobile, it doesn't look clean or readable if the text in the bullet points are super long.

Pro-tip 2: If you struggle to come up with hooks for your listicles, look for inspiration from popular YouTube titles, other posts, or blog articles on a platform like Medium. The more you study blog or YouTube titles the more your mind will start to think in a way that leads to more engagement.

Write A "Reasons Why" Listicle

I'm giving you some difficult ones for a reason.

The hook for this could have easily been "5 reasons why you should pursue real wealth," but it's not.

It starts with an attention-grabbing big idea.

"X is not about Y."

Then, it gives a list of benefits to help illustrate what X is in his own definition.

Then, it wraps it up with a last line that hits the main point.

You can see a clear transformation arc here.

Real wealth is not about money → Real wealth is about freedom.

The list in between paints an image in people's heads to make it that much more impactful.

Write A "Pain Point' Listicle

This checks almost all boxes.

How to, benefits, pain point.

"If you struggle with mental clarity" calls out people with a specific pain point.

The bullet list gives a quick "how to."

The last line concludes with a benefit and summary.

However, I could have also written the list to have pain points as the bullet list itself.

"If you struggle with:

  • Mental clarity
  • Too many distractions
  • Pulling yourself away from your phone

Write down all your thoughts. Organize them by priority. Sit down and focus."

My point with today's lesson wasn't to show you how to write a list.

It was to show you that most good social posts are mini stories.

They hint at a pain point.

They give you a solution, even if it's one sentence.

They illustrate some benefit of what life would look like if that pain point was solved.

This will come up more as we progress through the challenge, but for now:

  • Write at least 3 list posts with any idea you have
  • Attempt to use a pain point, solution, and benefit in at least one of the posts
  • Start to think of writing in terms of those 3 ingredients – the best posts aren't just tricks and tips, their micro transformations
