Today, I want to do the same thing, but slightly different.

See, social media is about experimenting with ideas and structures until one starts to outperform the rest.

When that happens, you lean into that idea or structure for a while to "ride the wave," then it inevitably stops working as well as it used to because everyone starts to copy it.

I'll give you a few examples:

On YouTube, dan joe made around 30-40 videos before one video on "The One Person Business" started going viral out of nowhere.

This was his chance to keep that momentum going for as long as it would last.

he began making more and more about all topics related to the one person business. he posted compilations of videos on the topic so people could watch them all in one place. he made multiple courses on the subject, so he had somewhere for the traffic to go.

It took him 2 years to get to 7,000 subscribers, and another year to get to 500,000+ subscribes because one single video blew up and all of his other videos started getting recommended all the time. At that point, saturation didn't matter. All he needed was to hit the jackpot once over the course of 3 years, and then maintain it. I firmly believe anyone can do that.

This is how social media growth happens. It's slow and linear until it goes exponential, then it dies down to a new baseline and repeats.

Right now, his baseline engagement is around 70,000 to 100,000 views. At the start, it was around 500-2500 views (because I was promoting it to the audience I had already built on Twitter). A year or so ago, my baseline was around 30,000 to 50,000 views.

As of rn, his YouTube channel is in a phase where nothing is going very viral. This happens every so often and most people quit during these times when they should focus on experimenting with how they create.

Another example:

On Instagram, he was just copy-pasting my best tweets of the week into an image format and posting them as single images.

Then, he started putting multiple tweets into a carousel (like a slideshow of images you see on IG). At the time, it seemed like Instagram was boosting carousels and he was able to grow from 4,000 followers to 250,000 relatively quick. 3-4 months.

Of course, that died down. People started spamming carousels and the strategy no longer worked.

he saw some people posting animations, so I hired an animator to turn my tweets into black and white animations that matched my brand. I recorded the tweets as voice-overs and sent them to the animator.

That's how he went from 250,000 followers to 1.2 million over the course of a few months. The secret is that he kept reposting my best content in the format that the platform was biasing at the time.

The content idea, structure, and format of the platform all play a role in how well you do.

Again, significant growth isn't linear. You often "blow up" overnight after months to years of experimentation.

One last example:

I noticed that "micro articles" were performing well on X. If you occasionally scroll through the timeline, you start to notice when something begins trending.

These are images that look like blog posts. A lot of text stuffed into an image. (I'll go over examples of these below).

Since the X algorithm (at the time) favored the amount of time people spent on a post, having an image with a lot of text on it sent engagement through the roof.

The first few micro articles that dan koe posted got over 1 million views and were among his highest performing content pieces.

However, that didn't last forever, and he returned to my normal posts while experimenting with other formats. Social media growth is active, not passive.

Option 2) Write One Micro Article

This option is a bit more difficult but very rewarding.

Choose an idea, then practice writing using the PAS framework (Problem → Amplify → Solution).

Illustrate the problem with a part of your personal story or directly state the problem. Then, amplify it by digging deep into how it impacts their life (use examples, metaphors, descriptions of the "low point"). Then, provide a few steps with explanations for how to overcome that problem.

Note: Unless you plan on including these as a part of your weekly content rotation, I wouldn't focus on this. Most people on social media do just fine focusing on short-form content.
