DAY 8 - THE TRUST MATRIX/HOW TO STAND OUT.

Welcome to day 8

Now that we've practiced writing various types of posts for a week, it's time to get to the fun stuff.

Here's one big point I want to get across:

Your content, and the quality of ideas you post over a long enough time period are what create a brand that people can't help but trust.

That's why we started with writing content.

Because frankly, that's all that really matters. There are people who have literally nothing written in their bio who have an outstanding brand. There are people without websites, LLCs, logos, or any cohesive brand design who have some of the best brands out there.

Why?

The content they've written over time.

What does the content do?

It builds trust.

And since money is a measure of trust, by building a personal brand, you gain quite a few advantages over other online businesses. You don't need to optimize your landing page copywriting. You don't need the greatest marketing in the world. Those things matter, but most people trust you enough to buy what you're selling without much friction because you have a track record of value.

So, how do we make sure our content builds trust through our content?

We focus on 3 pillars, I call this The Trust Matrix:

  • Growth – doing "what works" to attract people.
  • Authenticity – expressing your core beliefs.
  • Authority – displaying your expertise.

All of these overlap in one way or another. Specific posts aren't always "growth" posts or "authority" posts. They can be one, both, or all three.

Since we've already learned how to find and structure high-performing ideas, we will not focus on the "Growth" pillar. The past 7 days taught you what you need to know for that.

But now that we know how to write content, we need to make sure of two things:

  • People know who we are
  • People know what we do

Otherwise, you're just an account slinging out motivational quotes and tips. People have no reason to form a deeper connection with you or pay you. You blend in.

Authenticity

Your brand becomes authentic when you illustrate your story, core beliefs, and opinions over time in your content.

Sure, you can (and should) write posts that go over the entirety of your story. Those tend to do very well. Aside from that, you should try to weave personal experiences into ideas that you write about.

This doesn't have to be every day, but it's wise to try to weave something in at least a few times a week. If it's easier to do once a day, then do that. Remember, experiment.

You can use parts of your story and core beliefs in two different ways:

1) To reframe an idea

If I come across the idea of "you're supposed to be overwhelmed, it means you're learning," I can use my story to make that unique instead of copying the idea.

So I could say:

When I learn something new, I get overwhelmed.

I wanted to avoid it, but then I realized it should be any other way.

You're supposed to feel overwhelmed when you don't know how to do it yet.

I could go on, but you get the point.

2) As the topic itself

If I think over my past experiences and core beliefs, I land on a time when I fell off my fitness journey, would eat McDonald's for breakfast, and watch the office all day.

I could write about that experience, or I could turn it into some kind of lesson.

Now, thinking about your story is difficult if you don't know what parts of your story to write down.

And thinking about your "core beliefs" is often abstract and difficult to pin down when it's all in your head.

Authority

If you are taking this challenge, I'm assuming that you aren't trying to be the next big celebrity or OnlyFans model.

You're trying to build meaningful work and have a positive impact on others.

You're using your personal brand as a trust mechanism for your current business or one you want to start.

For that reason, the way you build authority is through education. You teach what you know to help people, attract customers who need your expertise, and build trust to earn some kind of income through the value you provide.

The thing is, there are so many creators who already teach what you want to teach. You can search for any skill on YouTube and find a ton of videos on any topic.

That's what trips up most beginners. They don't believe that people have a reason to follow them. Why would they learn from you when there are already plenty of people to learn from?

There are 3 solutions here.

1) Teach through a new lens

This is similar to reframing an idea through the lens of your story.

You take a unique idea you find, and you use it as a way to teach what you now from a new angle.

If I teach personal branding, many other people do too.

But if I come across the idea that "attention is the new oil" one day, I now have a unique way of teaching personal branding.

I could write a post like:

Attention is the new oil.

I don't mean dancing in front of a camera to get millions of views.

I mean putting your story, beliefs, and opinions online so people trust and resonate with you.

I'm just spit balling here.

2) Persuade the non-interested

I sound like I'm beating a dead horse because I talk about this so much in my content, but it is so important to internalize:

Most people on social media aren't intentionally trying to learn. If they were, they would physically search for a video, course, or article on the topic. And, you are being spread to random people on social media. You don't control who sees your content by typing in their interests and demographics into something like Facebook ads.

So, you need to frame your ideas as desirable to the average person.

If I want to talk about personal branding, I don't go straight into giving people instructions on how to pick their topics or create a bio. That's boring. It won't get enough reach to spread you to a new audience.

I start with a relatable pain point or desire.

As an example:

If you hate the thought of building someone else's dreams for the rest of your life, start a personal brand.

I could then go on to list the benefits of starting one, how low-risk it can be, and how you can sell almost anything from digital products to software to physical products.

In the example, that's a pretty broad pain point that many people relate with. It gives them a potential solution while positioning you as the authority who can help them with that. You may not be able to solve all their problems with one social media post, but that's why they follow you. That's why they click your link in bio. That's why they look for something that you are selling to help them solve their problem.

Mastering curiosity and desire by focusing on pain points, desires, and benefits in your content is how you help people become interested in your interests.

Building authority tends to come down to targeting pain points and providing some kind of solution. That way, people begin to see you as the person who can help with their goals.
