DAY 28 - HOW TO LAUNCH A PRODUCT

It's going to take some time to flesh out your product and write your landing page.

However, once you have your landing page ready, I'd recommend setting a launch date and starting to promote immediately.

On the last day of this challenge, we will recap the content ecosystem we talked about on day 10 of this challenge.

When you start your launch period, you aren't doing much extra work. You are simply promoting your product within the content you are already creating, while shifting your content topics to align more with the product you are selling.

This will make more sense as we learn about this.

Here's the problem:

Beginners don't realize that a launch is when you make the most money throughout the lifecycle of the product.

So, they spend a lot of time building the product, but they don't make any noise. They aren't loud enough. They don't have deadlines in place. Or they just stay quiet and promote occasionally leading to disappointment.

Over the past 5 years, I've developed a system that works seamlessly the content ecosystem we learned on day 10.

Before we begin, I'd recommend saving this Launch Strategy template here somewhere safe.

This promotion strategy assumes that you already have a weekly short-form and long-form content flow.

Short-form is obvious, it’s social media content that you post every day. A normal post.

Long-form can be a newsletter, YouTube video, or even a thread or carousel.

We are going to assume that you are posting short-form every day.

We are also going to assume that you are posting one long-form a week minimum.

Nuances & Tactics To Pay Attention To

Note: this is only one way to launch a product.

It's what I've done for the past few years. If you're a beginner, it's great for you. If you're seasoned, you probably have ways you can make this better.

I don't talk about paid ads, and I don't talk about specifics for something like a massive book launch where you're trying to hit NYT best seller status.

This is as simple and "lazy" as you can get if you are already posting content.

Now, you do not want to waste a product launch.

This is when you are going to make the bulk of your money (until you increase the price or run a special with the same strategy).

You want to exhaust any and all firepower you have:

1) Reposts, shares, and comments from your network.

You want as many eyes as possible during this period.

Reach out to people ahead of time (using what we learned on day 21) to build goodwill.

A few weeks before your launch, let your network know that you are going to be launching a product. Tell them it's important to you. Tell them they don't have to help, but if they're open to it, that any kind of share or engagement would mean the world.

2) Make sure you have scarcity baked into the promotion.

What is going to happen on the launch date?

Does enrollment close (like for a cohort)?

Does the price increase (early bird pricing ends)?

Do bonuses go away?

Do not just launch a product without having some form of benefit to those buying when it does launch.

You need scarcity to promote leading up to the launch over 3 weeks that pushes people over the edge to buy.

For most people launching a simple digital product, you will probably be increasing the price when it launches. You will have an early-bird discount.

3) This becomes more powerful as your audience grows on all platforms.

It’s good to focus on one platform, but once you have your content flow down, your promotions transfer seamlessly.

When I promote my product launch in my newsletter, all I have to do is read the promotion to my camera because my newsletter is my YouTube script.

When I plug my promotions under my short-form content, I just have to copy/paste both the content and promotion to other platforms.

I also screenshot the post and promotion and post them to my Instagram story.

You shouldn't worry about this if you are just starting out. Obviously you will make more sales if you have a larger audience across various platforms, but that is by no means necessary to make more than the average US salary.

4) Trust the f*cking process.

Sales will be slow in the first week.

They will pick up in the second.

They will go bonkers in the third.

You will question your sanity by how many sales you make in the last 2 days leading up to launch.

Stick this entire process out even if the first few promotions don’t bring much results.

All of this, of course, is also dependent on your audience size and how much traffic you can generate through people sharing your posts (through networking or paid means).

With 1000 followers, I could out earn someone with 50,000 if I was good at building a network of accounts with more followers than me that would strategically repost what I share (or affiliate for the product).

5) Plan 3 Weeks Ahead (For Awareness & Authority Building)

Set a launch date at least 3 weeks ahead of when you will start promoting.

If you are writing about topics unrelated to your product, that’s fine and encouraged, but we want to narrow our focus to the topic that your product is about for this 3-week period.

This will prime your audience's minds to be ready for the product when it drops.

Anything less than 3 weeks is not enough time for most of your audience to even know that you are launching a product. Not everyone sees your content or reads through it all. I still have people show up 2 weeks after a launch saying "I had no idea you had this product."

This product launch period is where your brand builds a lot of perceived authority. Selling something doesn't diminish you, it does the opposite.

Here’s a breakdown of the launch strategy:

Week 1 - Soft Promotion In Your Long-Form Content & Introducing More Specific Short-Form Posts

During week 1, you want to mention that you are launching a product in your newsletter or other long-form.

You do this at the top and bottom of your newsletter.

I tend to promote after my introduction, and in the “P.S.” at the end of my newsletter.

You can see an example in this newsletter. This is a soft promotion, I’m not hard selling, I’m just making them aware. This doesn't lead to many sales. It's just strategic awareness building. Most people don't buy something the first time they see it.

This is why I always stress the importance of newsletter-first content (to be turned in YouTube videos, podcasts, and a blog post that you can plug under your content.)

For BOTH your short-form and long-form posts, you will be basing them off of the most valuable topics, skills, and mindset around the topic of your product.

You can pull straight from your product if you want to for content ideas during this 3 week period.

Now, under your short-form posts, you are going to plug the link to your newsletter that contains the promotion. Do this once a day under your top-performing post for that day. Wait at least 1-2 hours before plugging.

This will lead to more email subscribers (so you can reach them with harder promotions), or they will see your product and buy it there.

So, if I sent out a newsletter on Substack or Beehiiv (which allows you to have a public "blog" online version of the article) I would plug that link to the article instead of the product itself. That way, people see the soft promotion inside the article. This is how you sell without selling, and while building trust first.

Remember, this is only week 1 of the strategy.

Week 2 - Hard Promotion In Your Long-Form Content & Plugging The Product Under Your Short-Form Posts

You are now in your second week of writing a newsletter and short-form content about your product.

Do the same thing as last week, but promote a bit harder in your newsletter in the same places. After the introduction and in the P.S. at the end.

Give a list of the problems your product solves and position your product as the solution.

This will lead to some sales depending on the size of your email list.

In your short-form content, instead of plugging your newsletter, you are going to plug the product and flex your scarcity mechanism. Do this once a day under your top-performing posts again.

If you don’t know how to promote, use this:

If you struggle to:

  • Common struggle 1
  • Common struggle 2
  • Common struggle 3

[Product name] enrollment closes on [date]. Enroll here: [link]

This will lead to more sales and make more people aware that you are selling a product.

You can use that template for both the promotion in the newsletter and in your short form content.

If you are posting something like Reels, TikToks, or on a platform where a reply doesn't get seen much, you have to get creative with this.

You can use something like Stans auto-dm feature for Instagram to send people a link to your product. You would write your promotion as the caption of the Reel or IG post.

For something like TikTok, I'm not really sure, as I've never done it and don't care to. But you can spend some time scrolling and studying how people promote their products on TikTok.

Week 3 - Hard Promotion & Direct Promotion In your Newsletters & In Your Short-Form Content

Week 3 is when everything starts to pick up.

You are going to do another hard promotion in your newsletter.

Be sure to remind them of either a price increase, enrollment closing in X days, or whatever else you use as scarcity.

You are still going to promote the product under one of your top-performing posts each day.

For the final 3 days:

You will make a direct post to the timeline on social media, not under your content.

Like this promotion.

Do this every day for those 3 days.

These won't get as much views or engagement as your other posts, but that's okay.

On the last day:

You are going to send out a promotion email using a solid copywriting framework like PASTOR. The headline will be something like “today is the final day.” You can use the same process we talked about in this article.

Then, you are going to send out a short email reminding people that the price increases or enrollment closes 4-6 hours before it does.

So, 2 emails on the last day.

We will talk about how to write these emails and social promotions tomorrow.

On social media, you are going to make another "last call" post.

In the replies of the post, or in your stories, this is where you can go into detail about what's inside, post screenshots of people who are excited to join, and answer questions related to the product.

I usually delete these after the promotion ends, so I can’t link an example :/

I trust that you can test and experiment for yourself.

That’s really it.

Don't beat yourself up if your launch doesn't meet expectations.

You can always rerun this process 2-3 months later if you improve the product and increase the price. Or when you launch another product.
