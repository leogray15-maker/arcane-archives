What you feed your body affects your physical health. Similarly, what you feed your mind affects your mental health.

Cleaning up what you feed your body is straightforward. A few less cookies, a bit more broccoli. But improving your mental diet isn't so obvious. Here are 3 ways you can do so:

1) Turn off the news

You're probably seeing the news every day.

To stay informed, right? What if I told you the news is destroying your mental health?

Not only does negative news affect you at the time you consume it, it can also affect you long after. A 2015 study found that people who watched just 3 minutes of negative news were 27% more likely to be unhappy 6 to 8 hours later, compared to those who watched positive news [1]. That was five years ago. The news has gotten far more negative since then...

The constant barrage of negative news also programs your brain that the world is a terrible place. My mom often says "the world's gone crazy..."

But why is the news so negative? Well, ever heard the old saying "if it bleeds, it leads"?

Your brain has a negativity bias. It cares more about negative events than positive events. It's a survival mechanism. Media companies know this. So they keep pushing negative news. They keep you hooked, coming back to "be informed", so they can make that sweet advertising and subscription money.

Stop consuming the news. You'll be happier. If an event is important, you'll hear about it.

2) Drop the Karens (and the Negative Nancys)

They say you are the sum of the 5 people you spend the most time with. This actually isn't quite accurate - it's more like everyone in your network (and their network too).

Well, if 2 of those people are pessimistic, that negativity is going to rub off on you. Negativity is incredibly toxic. Here's an example:

Long ago, I knew a guy (let's call him Mopey) who was extremely negative. He'd complain about (quite literally) anything and everything. Saying he was a downer is an understatement. His presence would suck the life out of every conversation he was in.

Mopey's toxic negativity cost his peers a lot of time. When he wasn't around, people complained about how much he complained about everything. Eventually I moved away (not because of Mopey). I don't have to deal with him anymore. But last I heard, Mopey is still as mopey as ever.

If you have a Mopey in your life, and they refuse to change, do whatever you can to cut them out. If they're a coworker or family member, this can be challenging. I get it. Otherwise, remove them from your life. Stop feeling bad for them and start standing up for YOURSELF.

3) Stop the negative self-talk

When you say something about yourself, your subconscious mind listens. As you keep saying that something, two things start happening:

  • it accepts what you said as truth
  • it motivates you to act consistently with that "truth"

I went to Georgia Tech, an engineering college. It's a great school, but it had a problem - a culture of self-deprecation (making negative "jokes" about ourselves). We thought of it as just a coping mechanism. Tech is legendarily challenging, so we constantly made "jokes" about ourselves. Georgia Tech also has a huge issue with depression among the student body. There's a relationship there.

When you talk or think badly about yourself, your brain starts to accept it as truth. So, it motivates you to act consistently with the truth. Your actions change. You start becoming the person you said you were. Your happiness and your life start going downhill.

Unhappy with yourself, you start talking WORSE about yourself... and the cycle continues. Voilà, depression. Here's an easy "hack" to fix this: Every time you say (or think) something negative about yourself, immediately say (or think) the exact OPPOSITE. For example, "I can't" becomes "I can."

Be good to yourself.
