Day 4

Awareness Levels

You do not talk to different people the same way.

And this is true at 2 levels of the Copywriting process:

  • When creating and marketing your product.
  • When writing your Copy.

And to help us do that, we use the 5 levels of awareness' that advertising legend Eugene Schwartz came up with in the 60s.

His premise was simple: Having a great product is not enough. You must meet your prospects where they're at in their journey.

The awareness scale lets you assess how familiar your audience is with their problem and your solution. It helps tailor your copy to match your audience's current state of mind.

Here are the five levels and what they mean:

Unaware:

  • Don't know they have a problem (or are not honest about it).

Problem aware:

  • Know they have a problem to solve, but don't know there's a solution to it.

Solution aware:

  • Know there's a solution to their problem, but don't know which exactly.

Product aware:

  • Know about your solution, but don't know everything it does or how well it does it.

Most aware:

  • Know about your product and what it does.

1. An unaware' prospect has never heard about the problem, the solution, and your product and brand. They're the most detached from you. And the chance of them becoming a buyer is slim.

EX: A 60 year-old cattle raiser <> online copywriting business.

They don't know anything about it.

2. A problem aware' prospect knows that there's a challenge, opportunity, or problem, but they don't know what the solution is, nor that your product and brand exist.

EX: A woman who just had her first child <> a weight loss supplement.

For the first time in her life, she's taken on excess weight, and she doesn't know what to do about it.

3. A solution aware' prospect faces a problem and has tried different solutions in the past, to no avail. They don't know yours yet.

EX: A watch enthusiast <> your new brand of Swiss luxury watches.

Here I'm taking the example of someone who's not facing a problem per se (but the rule still applies).

4. A product aware' prospect knows about you and your product, but they've never purchased it.

EX: A business owner <> a tax software.

At first they filed their taxes by hand, but now that their business has grown, they're considering their options.

5. Most aware' customers are good buyers of yours. They know and appreciate you well.

EX: Many online marketers buy every new book or course from their favourite teacher because they know it's going to give them an edge.

Each awareness level requires a different approach.

At the level of the products

A product never exists on its own, separate from a context.

You create products in relation to each other. And each one has its own place on the awareness scale.

The idea is to get people from 1 to 5: From unaware to most aware.

You structure your products according to this scale.

This is why you have 'front-end' products and 'back-end' products.

Also known as Top of Funnel (TOF) Middle of Funnel (MOF) Bottom of Funnel (BOF) products.

Front-end/TOF products 'convert: They turn non-buyers into buyers.

They are the first point of entry for your prospects to become customers.

Back-end/MOF-BOF products 'monetize:' They incite buyers to spend more with you.

They build loyalty with your brand.

Now ask yourself: What are Front-end products selling? What the goal?

It's NOT revenue... But a first step into your world.

Which is why you want quality volume on the front-end...

...And revenue on the back-end.

This changes the way you sell, whether it's a front-end product or a back-end product.

Front-end: have a strong promise that's 'too good to refuse,' the key word being 'Abundance.'

Back-end: offer credibility and results, the key word being 'Certainty."

Here's an example:

  • Free book on the front-end with a $2.99 shipping fee.
  • $298 upsell on the back-end
  • $12k/year coaching program at BOF.

You wouldn't write the same copy for each.

Quick note: you want your front-end product to feel like a bargain, but you still want your prospect to spend a little amount with you.

As surprising as it may seem, there's a world of difference in commitment between grabbing something for free and spending as little as $2.99 on something.

The first scenario builds an audience of 'triers' (aka leeches) while the second one builds a list of buyers.

Now here's how to use the awareness levels in your copy.

At the level of the copy

First, look at what type of product you're selling, whether it's a front-end or back-end product.

Then you must decide which awareness level you want to target.

If you're on the front-end, with paid traffic (like ads) for example, you can address every awareness level. Inside this pool you will have people who've never heard about you or what you sell... And people who know you.

You have to choose the bigger market, the one with the largest volume and hence, better potential.

If you're on the back-end, you will only talk to clients, or qualified audiences.

So usually, no need to go the unaware route, unless you're selling something entirely new to them (they don't know they need it.)

Now, here are the examples from earlier and how you could go about it.

Remember: You want to meet people at their current understanding and guide them to deeper awareness and/or engagement with the subject.

1. For the unaware, introduce the issue and focus on building general awareness. Use broad, relatable contexts and stay away from technical details or specific solutions.

EX: Say you want to pitch your Copy skills to the 60 years old cattle raiser...

You could tell him how sharing his daily life on the farm and how he looks after his animals on the Internet could help his business.

It would show his customers how high-quality his meat and dairy products are and allow him to charge higher prices.

Or he could go for a direct-to-consumer business model and increase his margins.

To catch his attention, you could show him how other farmers are doing it. In other words, tell him a story.

  • 2. For the problem aware, acknowledge the problem directly. And hit them with a 'proclamation' like a shocking fact to get their attention.

EX: Back to the woman who just had her first child....

Here, you could tell her how Japanese women always return to their pre-pregnancy weight... Thanks to copious amounts of green tea.

You happen to sell a weight-loss supplement that combines green tea extracts with caffeine but you don't mention that yet.

  • 3. For the solution aware, you can tease a 'secret.' It'll allow you to compare and contrast different solutions.

EX: Let's say you've launched your own microbrand of luxury Swiss watches and want to market it to watch collectors...

You could tease an insider secret about how most 'luxury' watch brands purchase cheap movements from China, barely modify them and yet claim their movements are built 'in-house..."

...And position your watches as the more honest and sensible choice.

Or you could rave about the huge markup jewellers are making on watches. And how your direct-to-consumer model allows you to offer the same quality a lot cheaper.

  • 4. For the product aware, a simple 'problem-solution' cascade will work. Identify your prospect's most pressing issue and follow it up with an easy, effective solution (your product.)

EX: You're selling a tax SaaS to small business owners.

Tell them how filing taxes takes too long. How complicated it is. How a minor reporting mistake can cost you $$$. And how big of a pain in the ass it is.

And offer to try your tax software for free for the next month.

  • 5. For the most aware, a strong 'promise' can be enough to get them interested.

EX: You sell a Copywriting course to online marketers.

Promise them the insider knowledge you'll reveal inside is the key to unlocking multi 6-figures launches. Back it up with proof and you're set.

  • 6. For your customers, a straightforward 'offer' will do.

EX: Your customer sees his yearly membership in your online community coming to an end.

Offer a special discount for renewal or a gift (one of your courses, a free call, etc.)

As you can see from these 6 examples, the more aware your prospect is, the more direct you should be.

Here are some general guidelines:

  • Assess your prospect's current level of awareness before engaging with him: research and talk directly with your audience
  • Make sure your first sentences confirm you're addressing the right level of awareness
  • Focus on your audience's needs and perspective
  • Use appropriate language and depth to match each level of awareness: don't mention 'AIDA' to people who don't know the difference between Copywriting and 'copyrighting'
  • Make sure the path to the next level of awareness is clearly laid out and makes sense
  • Increase complexity and specificity gradually

An Intro To 'Leads'

The goal of this course is to gradually introduce you to key concepts of effective copywriting...

...Which is why we're currently focusing on the 'backbone' of Copy instead of the 'writing.'

So without getting into too much detail, I'd like you to understand the importance of a Copywriting 'lead.'

A Copywriting 'lead' is the opening section of your ad or sales letter. Your first 200-1000 words.

Its sole purpose is to capture your reader's attention and entice them to continue reading.

An effective lead piques curiosity, addresses a pain point, or offers a compelling benefit that resonates with your target audience. It sets the tone for the rest of the copy and creates a desire to learn more.

Leads are crucial because they determine whether a reader will engage with your content or move on.

There are 6 types of leads and the book 'Great Leads from Michael Masterson (Mark Ford) & John Forde will tell you everything you need to know about them.

And if you pay attention, you'll notice I've described all 6 of them above without mentioning them while using relatable, real-life examples.

Because my goal is to give you a gradual understanding of the Copywriting process without drowning you in writing templates.

We'll be covering a lot of ground during this 31-day training, hence why I stand by my recommendation of not reading books for now.

But once you and I are finished, I highly recommend you give 'Great Leads' a thorough read.

For now, simply remember that your copy needs to address your prospects where they currently are.
