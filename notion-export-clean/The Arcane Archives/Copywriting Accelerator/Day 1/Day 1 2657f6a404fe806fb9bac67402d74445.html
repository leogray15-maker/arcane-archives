...And before we get started

Let me explain why copy is the hardest thing to start.

Learning copywriting can be an impossible task. But there's a reason behind that difficulty, and thus a solution.

And the reason is that when you start out, you have no model, no reference.

You don't know what's up, you don't know what's down. You can't differentiate between North and South. And you have no idea what's right or wrong.

See, in this training, you will often hear me talking about 2D or 3D environments, a concept I don't talk about in the discord simply because it's unique. Things like maps, roads, space. This is for two reasons:

  1. You can only understand copywriting when you see it as a dynamic system. And the best way to understand a system is to visualize it. Hence the visual language I use.
  1. Persuasion can also be understood as a process, a movement. You move people towards a direction you've chosen. Emotions are also kinetic: you say that art 'touches' you or 'moves' you. And to have movement, you must have space.

This whole training is about establishing the space we'll be evolving in, along with a few key references and models.

This is why I will ask you to stop reading books, and concentrate on the training at hand.

That's because books are useful only when they're being considered inside an already established space, with correct points of reference...

I.e. you're probably not ready to understand them yet.

Because they will suggest new spaces, new geometries, new systems. And this will come in direct contradiction to other books, or even this very training.

You'll be left with more questions than answers.

With this course we're establishing the FIRST space in which you will evolve. And what's great is that it's 90% correct. Meaning that it will work 9 out of 10 times.

I suggest you start by simply following this training, without reading other material. And focus on developing your mental map. You can even use a whiteboard if this allows you to really 'see' the copywriting process.

On that note, let us begin the exercise and the reason for doing so.

Write 1 letter a day

Now the best way to establish a working model is to start with something you know. And with copywriting, the best starting point is the letter.

You know the piece of paper you write on, and then send through the mail to your grandma.

Well, sales letters are based on epistolary correspondence. (If you don't know what that means, search it up) It's the ancestor of our modern landing pages and VSLs.

This is why it's great to start your copywriting journey not with emails, long form sales letters, or VSLs... but with simple letters to people you know.

This is going to be a significant part of your training, and the basis for the assignments I'll give you.

Mastering the correspondence is the secret weapon to mastering copywriting. Because the main objective is to understand how you talk to people 1 on 1. Conversationally. Like human beings.

When you know how to write clearly and conversationally then the doors of conversion will open up for you.

This is why you will be tasked with writing a personal letter a day. It doesn't need to be a long letter. It must follow these three rules:

  • It should be personal. Meaning that it shouldn't sound like copy or marketing. It should look like a real letter that you would really send to someone you love.
  • It should end with a call to action. Meaning that you will ask something of the person you write to.
  • It should follow the prompt I give you in each exercise sheet.

There is no 'right or wrong' to this exercise. It's all about:

  • Getting into the mindset of writing to one single person.
  • Getting those 'writing reps' in and making it a daily habit.
  • Practising clear and concise writing.
  • Implementing what you're learning along the way.

Now on to the next practise.

Read 1 piece of copy a day

The second best way to establish a working model is to accumulate good reference points.

The problem with copywriting today is that you will see too many terrible examples of copy written by wannabe writers who couldn't persuade a dog to chew a bone.

This once again blurs the line between good and bad copy. And leaves you wondering what the hell winning copy looks like.

This is why I will also give you proven swipes to analyse. You don't have to hand-write them but if you're feeling special, go ahead. Just read them carefully with a pen in hand, and dissect them. Notice patterns. Important moments. Significant words.

Become so accustomed to great copy that it infuses your unconscious mind.

For your convenience, I've included 4 questions to guide you as you analyse the daily swipes.

  • What's the main idea behind this piece of copy?
  • Who is it directed to?
  • What emotion does it play on?
  • Why do you think it worked?

You'll get the best ROI by studying the swipes in full. But some of them are long - up to 40 or 50 pages...

...So if you don't have the time or if you find it too daunting, you can get away with analysing the 'lead', which is the first 200-1000 words.

You'll see I haven't included my own analysis, which is intentional. To put it simply, the goal of this course is to give you the tools to understand Copy at a deeper level as you progress through the material. And if I were to give you a thorough analysis, I would be bypassing this process.

Your goal is not to be 'right' or 'wrong' about your analysis. It's to study great Copy consistently and hone your intuition. Do with what you know and feel first, and you'll see that as the days pass you notice more and more elements of persuasion. Here's another thing. You don't have to like the swipes... Nor do you have to be willing to buy the product... But the reason I've included them into your training is because they've all been successful.

And your only task is to understand the reasons behind their success, so you can open yourself up to other techniques and methods, and become a better Copywriter as a result.

Find 1 idea a day.

Finding one idea a day can seem weird, but it's a crucial part of the Copywriting trifecta.

It's about training your brain to find new and exciting ways of thinking about old problems.

Finding an idea can seem difficult at first, but it's nothing crazy. Your idea doesn't have to be linked to Copy.

It can be as simple as:

  • A new angle for a sales letter
  • A cool headline
  • A new product you'd buy if you could
  • A new convincing way to present a guarantee

etc.

It can really be anything.

Not all of your ideas will be interesting or valuable. And that's ok.

It's about quantity and repetition.

Because the truth is that you'll only find a handful of truly great ideas in your Copywriting career...

Just like great artists only come up with a handful of masterpieces, or athletes pull off a handful of significant moves in their career.

People remember the wins but they don't see all the failed attempts it took to get there.

So get yourself a brand new notebook, and write down 1 idea or more every single day. Get those reps in.

Who knows, it may allow you to find a million-dollar Big Idea.

Most beginners struggle with this exercise so let me give you a practical example.

Hypothetical Situation:

I was having lunch with a good friend of mine who's a private wealth manager. We were talking about how most people still spend a lot on leisure despite the rising costs of living.

Going to the movies, for instance, has become twice as expensive in just a few years.

He said a lot of his clients have inherited a lot of money from their parents, from savings to real estate...

....And they're using their newfound wealth to increase their standard of living.

He then casually said this.....

"Boomers are the richest generation to ever walk the earth. And since more of them are passing away, we're right in the middle of the largest transfer of wealth from one generation to the next."

That's when my light bulb went on. And I explained to him how this fact could position wealth management training as the opportunity of a lifetime.

"Take your cut from the largest generational wealth transfer in history."

(Wealth managers like him make money from commissions on successful investments.)

The funny thing is that he never even considered it. He saw the fact, but not the opportunity.

And that's exactly what I mean when I say that ideas are 'found' instead of 'generated.'
