Balance Is Found Through Cycles Of Intensity

we love to glorify monk mode and intensity.

I love a good old monk mode when you feel pulled to go all-in...

laser-focused deep work, getting a years worth of work done in a month, losing sleep because the ideas won’t stop flowing, and riding the wave of dopamine and intrinsic drive

but sustainability has to be taken into account.

If you stay in a state of intensity or optimization for too long, you hit a point of diminishing returns on quality of life.

When I started optimizing too much, life lost it's quality of life.

Things became stressful. Everything outside of my normal routine was seen as a burden.

If I didn’t control every little thing about my life, I couldn’t enjoy it for what it is.

here is a part of one of the philosophies where I describe how I felt...

"it's just a matter of resilience, which. by definition means that you will have to get up when you are struck down.

when in reality, the definition is simply standing up after you've been knocked down.

and sometimes, you can't control this knockdown period.

it can last months, especially when you've burnt yourself out working 14 hour days for a while.

the cortisol will build so high that you will go into a state of forced relaxation, but that relaxation will not be relaxing as you will want to get back to work, but you just cannot bring yourself to do it no matter what.

and a depressed person is simply a person who doesn't fulfill their promises.

I remember just sitting outside in the dark, trying to muster up an idea with my foggy brain, I was way too burnt out to even think.

I'd just stay outside, sip and spit out water wishing I could just grind some more, but at the same time wishing i could just rest.

However, These "Monk Mode" Cycles Are Needed.

When you transition out of these high-intensity states...

you operate from a higher baseline on whatever you exponentially improved.

you see, this is how it works for me...

My Cycle Of Diminishing Returns...

  1. I start working on something.
  1. I LOVE the thing I'm working on.
  1. All I think about is the thing I'm working on, getting constant new ideas...
  1. I work on that project for a certain amount of time.
  1. I start to lose all drive towards that project, I procrastinate EVERYTHING to get away from it.

and that's when you know that you have your diminishing returns.

once again, back to this graph.

I start something new, and I fall in love with it...

then I start to hate it, and enter the valley of despair.

then I acknowledge that I entered the valley of despair, or I got "burnt out"

then comes the slope of enlightenment... which comes from knowing that you reached peak of mount stupid at first, and that you're in this cycle.

and there you have sustainability, which leads to consistency.

but here's the main point...

this cycle will always happen, but the one thing you CAN change, is that you find out where the point of diminishing returns are.

and THEN that's when you take the rest from the monk mode...

then after you've rested for long enough, you'll have a DRIVE to work again.

like when you're at the holidays, but you just want to go home.

you will have that feeling with work.

and that's your mental reset.

it's like da vinci when he got burnt out from painting for 14 hour days...

and then he started painting again in his "monk mode" season.

everyone experiences this cycle, don't think you're more disciplined than da vinci.

The Scientific Method

Your body is a project. Your health is a project. Your relationships are a project. Your business is a project. All of these have more projects embedded in them.

The “Project Mindset” is powerful.

Projects imply a desired outcome (improvement), steps to completion (goals and metrics), and actions that will move you closer to completion (priorities).

They also imply failure. Not all experiments are successful.

When you think of this as a project, it is easier to detach from the outcome.

You see failure as a chance to iterate and improve.

Failure is feedback on what to improve. Looping back to awareness — you cannot improve what you are not aware of.

You need to come into your experiments with an open and curious mind. This will give room for questioning. Curiosity is fueled by questions. Question:

What would life look like if I tried this thing I’ve never tried before?

the point of life IS to experiment (not In a gay way, but if you're into that, go on)

You will naturally feel the pull to dive into the unknown. If you don’t, keep asking questions until you feel energized. That is your call to adventure. A call to become an explorer of the unknown. A call to start actualizing your potential.
