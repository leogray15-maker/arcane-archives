Up until recently, stress has been demonized. We're constantly told to lighten up and calm down. Toxic positivity gurus, Facebook moms, and even doctors keep telling us we need to minimize stress as much as possible.

But here's the truth - not all stress is bad.

Acute (short-term) stress and chronic (long-term) stress have different effects on our performance and health. Acute stress is good for you in many ways. Chronic stress is bad for you.

Acute stress

When something triggers your stress response, your body and mind start changing. A few of those changes are:

Your heart rate increases, sending more oxygen through your brain and the rest of your body. This improves your mental and physical performance.

Your motivation is (usually) increased. When we're stressed, we often want to find a way to overcome the thing causing our stress.

Adrenaline is released. This improves learning. How? When an event triggers a large adrenaline release, our brain thinks "hey, that event must have been important!" It remembers the event better than one that doesn't cause an adrenaline release.

A while back, a client told me he had a problem - his performance improves when he's angry. He felt like his performance was limited when he wasn't in a state of heightened emotions. I advised him to think of it in the opposite way - it's not that he's going from 80% to 100% when he's mad, he's going from 100% to 120%.

Having that extra gear you can engage when things get real is a good thing.

So, short-term stress has positive effects. But what happens when you face repeated short-term stress - or worse, long-term stress? That's when things get bad.

Chronic stress

Unlike acute stress, chronic stress has virtually zero benefits. Sure, you could say that going through long-term stress like a bad childhood or a terrible job made you mentally stronger. But that's another discussion entirely. It's based on the individual making a positive out of a negative.

Instead, chronic stress has a long list of negative impacts on your mental and physical health, including:

  • anxiety
  • insomnia
  • depression
  • poor focus
  • weight gain
  • eating disorders
  • irritability and anger
  • lowered immune system
  • low sexual desire (and maybe desirability)

... and many, many more

If you're like most people, you're suffering from chronic stress. It's not a matter of if you're stressed - it's a matter of how much stress you have at any given moment.

Here are 3 things you can do to reduce your chronic stress:

1) Get better sleep

Low sleep quality and high-stress levels often go hand-in-hand. If you're a high-stress person, chances are you're not getting enough sleep at night.

"But I don't have time to sleep more..." I hear you think. You don't have time to not sleep more. Next week, I'll write an article sharing a nightly routine that will help you get more sleep.

2) Meditate every day

Meditation is one of the most powerful habits you can possibly build. Regular meditation leads to certain regions of your brain changing volume, which in turn leads to you having better emotional control, focus, and clarity of mind.

People who meditate regularly report less stress and more peace, gratitude, and clarity. It's pretty incredible how doing nothing for a few minutes a day can change your life.

3) Cut things (or people) out

If you're constantly stressed out, you might need to do more than add meditation and sleep... you might need to subtract a thing or two from your life.

Think about the things stressing you out right now. They could be things you have to do or things you want to do. They could even be people. Is the stress that thing (or person) is causing you really worth it? Is there a light at the end of the tunnel? Is it getting any better as the weeks and months pass?

If that job has been stressing you out for months with no end in sight, it's time to start considering other options.

If a person around you isn't changing despite your best efforts to help them change, it's time to start thinking about distancing yourself from them.

If your agency is taking years off your life without adding enough dollars to your bank account, it's time to explore new business ideas.
