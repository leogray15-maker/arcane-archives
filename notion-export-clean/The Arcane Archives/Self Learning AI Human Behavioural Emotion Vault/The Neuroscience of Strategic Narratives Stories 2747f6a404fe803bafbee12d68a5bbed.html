The Neuroscience of Influential Strategic Narratives and Storylines

By Gregory S. Seese, Psy.D. & Kendall Haven, Ph.D.

Editor's Note

The following article presents a case for creating better stories to influence target audiences. The paper delves into the psychology of how people learn and remember stories and the affect a proper story structure can have on an audience.

Military Information Support Operations (MISO), formally known as Psychological Operations (PSYOP), are planned operations to convey selected information and indicators to foreign audiences to influence their emotions, motives, objective reasoning, and ultimately the behavior of foreign governments, organizations, groups, and individuals. The purpose of MISO is to induce and reinforce foreign attitudes and behavior favorable to the originator's objectives (FM 3-53, 2013).

Essential to influencing attitudes and behavior is a powerful message that resonates with the intended Target Audience (TA) and moves them to take action. Simple narratives alone do not tend to influence others, especially in today's message saturated environment. Well known to anyone with a background in the social and behavioral sciences, general attitude alone is a poor driver and predictor of behavior. For attitudes to influence behavior, they must be specific, personal, and relevant (Myers, 2011).

This is typically the challenge for anyone trying to persuade and influence. To change attitudes and behavior, a message must be incorporated into the conscious mind and memory of a TA. To accomplish this, a message (think of the message as a payload) needs a powerful and effective delivery system. The missing component (delivery system) is a storyline that engages and holds the attention of the TA, creates personal relevance and context, and causes them to identify, empathize, and adopt the beliefs (subcomponents of attitudes) of the character(s).

This paper will summarize the neural and cognitive science behind the use of a specific story structure as the most appropriate and effective delivery system for influence messaging and will propose a specific developmental methodology for merging MISO messaging into delivery story structures.

Effective Story Structure

In practice, only a tiny few stories are likely to have any lasting impact on listeners – to change beliefs, attitudes, and behavior. Converging multi-disciplinary research into effective story structure conducted over the past several years has shown that those stories that do have long-term impact on listeners share a common core narrative architecture (Haven, 2014). The structural elements that define that specific architecture differentiate effective stories from all other narratives.

Research through the accumulated corpus of international folklore suggests that these core elements of effective stories are global (McAdams 2006; Pinker 1997; Kotulak 1999; Zwaan et al 1998), even though their expression can include culturally specific biases and trends. We posit that stories capable of achieving the MISO mission adhere to, and take advantage of, those structural elements, while (and this is very important) tailoring their structure and content to the specific TA they aim to influence.

Neural Story Science behind the Structure

The following section presents a brief summary of the science evidence, findings, experimental results collected, compiled, and presented in Haven's 2009 and 2014 books.

The Neural Story Net

First proposed by evolutionary biologists, the following concept has been extensively tested by developmental psychologists and other neural scientists. (See Haven, 2009 and 2014.) For 150,000 years before writing existed (That is, before any alternate informational archival system other than human memory), humans relied on story and on storytelling to convey and to archive (in human memory) essential histories, concepts, facts, beliefs, and attitudes. That long-term dependency has evolutionarily hardwired human brains to understand, and to make sense of, incoming narrative and experiential information in specific story terms.

The story-creating/understanding "make sense" regions of the human brain are linked from before birth into a fixed neural net tasked with interpreting incoming narrative and experiential information.

That fixed neural hardwiring of sub-regions forms a dominant "Neural Story Net" that connects and co-fires those brain centers responsible for creating the specific core informational elements of story. A significant part of what the brain does for the conscious mind is structure experience into story form.

This Neural Story Net (NSN) interprets incoming information (makes story-based assumptions and inferences, restructures, reinterprets, selectively alters or ignores information, augments, etc.) before the information reaches the conscious mind so that the information received by the conscious mind is a "highly processed" self-created, story-based version of the original incoming information. The Neural Story Net lies between the conscious mind and the external world. (Haven, 2009 & 2014, Pinker, 2000; Newquist, 2004; Kotulak, 1999)

The Make Sense Mandate

One critical function of the Neural Story Net is to Make Sense of incoming narrative and experiential information – even if doing so forces the mind to revise, alter, augment, or disregard portions of the raw input information. If the brain can't make sense out of incoming information, it tends to discount and ignore it.

We require that it Makes Sense. We'll create (mentally invent) what we have to create to make it make sense by using such mental tools as cause & effect sequencing, temporal sequencing, centering around a common theme, character analysis, etc.

From the wide spectrum of research (of which the above are a tiny sample), we can safely conclude that the informational elements controlling how the Neural Story Net performs its Make-Sense Mandate exactly correspond to the core informational elements of the Haven (2014) story structural model.

Engagement and Attention

Beyond providing a vehicle for the essential function of sense making, an effective story structure must also provide explicit tools to control engagement and attention.

Story engagement is an essential gateway to narrative influence. Engagement has a mandatory emotional component. The informational elements of our structural model represent those elements and techniques used by tellers to establish and to manipulate reader/viewer engagement.

Based on recent EEG laboratory experiments (Haven 2013, 2014), we can comfortably apply the following definitions:

  • Engagement: emotionally laden attention (attention plus a strong degree of personal emotional investment)
  • Attention: dedicated mental focus over time

No engagement, no attention, and the potential for influence is lost.

The Eight Essential Elements of Effective Story Structure

Through extensive lab experiments (Haven 2013 and 2014) the eight elements of the Haven story model (Haven 2013 and 2014) were found to be both excellent tools for both controlling and for predicting TA engagement with a specific storyline. This research was far from the first to address neural response to story or effective story structure. However, it was the first to successfully bridge those two disparate worlds so that model elements directly corresponded to neural TA responses.

With that background summary development, the Eight Essential Elements of the model of effective story structure we propose for MISO use are:

Table I. Essential Informational Elements of the Story Structural Model

  1. Characters: Design and identity of the story characters that populate essential character role positions.
  1. Traits: Selected elements of character description used to control receiver attitude toward, and relationship to, story characters.
  1. Goal: What a character needs/wants to do/get in a story.
  1. Motives: The sets of drivers that make a goal important to a character. Goals and motives are the primary source of story suspense. Motive Matching is a primary tool for creating TA identity with, and empathy for, specific story characters.
  1. Conflicts & Problems: The sets of obstacles that stand between a character and an established goal. "Problem" is the general term and represents anything that blocks (even temporarily) a character from goal achievement. "Conflict" is a subset of Problems that block a character by placing that character in opposition to some other sentient entity in the story.
  1. Risk & Danger: The likelihood of failure (risk) and the consequences of failure (danger) created by problems and conflicts that a character must face. Risk & danger is the primary source of story excitement.
  1. Struggles: The sequence of events a character undertakes (struggle) to reach a goal highlighted by the climax scene (confrontation with the last & greatest obstacle) and the resolution scene.
  1. Details: The character, sensory, scenic, and event specific descriptors used to direct and control receivers' story imagery.

Story Influence Model

The Eight Essential Elements of effective story structure allow MISO Planners to control engagement, to hold TA attention, to ensure that the story will make sense and that it will create sufficient context, relevance, and understanding. However, these elements do not, themselves, exert story influence.

Recent work by Haven and others (Haven 2013 and 2014) has developed the first specific computational model of story influence potential and of the story elements controlling that potential.

Information, alone, rarely changes reluctant minds. It does not alter values, attitudes, and beliefs. Our research confirms findings by others that humans interpret new information to support existing beliefs and attitudes. The one thing that replaces (modifies) a story-based belief...is a better story. (Haven, 2013, 2014)

Thus, the process of narrative message based influence needs to be approached from a story structural basis.

Figure 1 shows the core narrative story elements that are most directly involved in controlling story influence potential and that provide explicit entry points for MISO messaging into story structures. Note that, while all of the Eight Essential Elements are needed to control engagement, attention, relevance, context, and understanding, not all directly affect a story's potential to influence. These informational variables form the core of our Influence Model.

Figure 1. The Structural Story Influence Line

[Main Character] → [Goal] → [Antagonist] → [Climax] → [Resolution] With [Motive] feeding into Goal, and [Climax Character] and [Residual Resolution Motive] affecting the outcome.

As shown, we experimentally found that story influence hinges on the TA's perception of four key character positions (Haven 2013, 2014). These are:

  • Main Character: The character that is central to the story's purpose and flow. It is the Goal of this character that is resolved at the story's conclusion. For most (but certainly not all—see Methodology Demonstration below) stories, the TA is induced to identify with this character and to view the world through this character's eyes.
  • Identity Character: The Identity Character does not hold a fixed structural position on the Story Influence Line and, thus, does not appear in Figure 1. However, the Identity Character—that story character with whom the TA most closely identifies—is key to story influence. The TA will evaluate the story's meaning and its influence by assessing the story through the eyes of this Identity Character. It is essential that you control your TA's Identity Character so that you can control the impact of the story on that audience.

Key to that identification process is Motive Matching. If the driving motives of a character match those of the TA, the audience is powerfully induced to identify with the story character. Motives are the most effective tool for controlling how a TA's decides their affinity for (or rejection of) story characters. It is valuable to ensure that the stated and implied motives of the identity character (that story character with whom you want the TA to identify most) match the core values and beliefs of the TA.

  • Antagonist: the physical embodiment of the greatest problem/obstacle that blocks the Main Character. While it is desirable – and far more powerful – it is not essential for the Antagonist to be represented by a living story character. Where there is conflict, a physical antagonist can always be found. However, some problems do not easily lend themselves to representation by a single character.
  • Climax Character: While unfamiliar to most story analysts, the position of Climax Character is the true story power position. At the moment of story climax the Main Character will confront for the final time the story Antagonist. The character who determines how that scene resolves itself is the Climax Character – the one story character with the power to determine the Climax's outcome.

Experimentally, we find that audiences subconsciously hold the Climax Character – or any likely or potential Climax Characters – responsible for the story's resolution. You can use story structural techniques to force a story character into the position of Climax Character makes that character responsible for the story's resolution in the minds of the TA.

Beyond these key character positions lies the story's resolution – a statement of whether or not the Main Character actually reaches their stated Goal, combined with how that character feels about this resolution.

From their emotional reaction to the three key character positions and to the resolution, the TA is left with a Residual Resolution Emotion (RRE) – how the TA feels about how the story ended. This RRE is key to creating belief, attitude, and behavioral change – that is, to influence. Shaver et al (2001) Taxonomy of Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary Emotions provides an excellent model in regards to RRE selection.

Proposed MISO Methodology

Effective stories are audience specific. That is, the Prior Knowledge, the shared beliefs and attitudes, and the common issues and concerns of a specific Target Audience will affect the way in which their NSN's will interpret and make sense of an incoming story delivery vehicle. The elements of a story must be tailored to engage, and to create relevance for, each specific audience.

The methodology we propose does not begin with consideration of the story. As a MISO delivery vehicle, the story cannot be developed until the TA and the specific messaging (the payload) have been articulated and developed. The story will then surround and carry that messaging.

Summary of the Storyline Narrative Methodology

  1. Target Audience Analysis During Phase 2 of the PSYOP Process (FM 3-05.301, 2007), profiles are developed of the TA demographics (age, economic status, education, religion, etc), psychographics (attitudes, values, interests, personality, etc) and cultural and environmental information that provide keys to the TA's Prior Knowledge and, thus, to context. Their vulnerabilities (goals, needs, wants, and desires, sources of pain), susceptibilities (risks/rewards of desired behavior), and internal (beliefs, values) and external conditions (situations, events) all provide keys to relevance.
  1. Influence Message (Theme Statement and objectives) Theme and message development may be broken into sequential sub-steps: A. Desired Outcome/Behavior: What do you need to accomplish with this story? What change do you expect to achieve? This is what the TA is to do or not to do. This is the prime metric of success. B. Knowledge's and beliefs: What does the TA need to know and believe to engage in the desired behavior? Knowledge objectives are the information and facts the TA is to know in regards to the desired behavior (statistics, facts, and other information the TA would find motivating or important), and belief objectives are the associated attitudes, values, and beliefs (Seese, 2014). What the TA needs to believe will be resonated in the themes. C. Theme Message: In one short, declarative statement, what message do you hope to implant in the TA's active memory? Think bumper sticker slogans. Think folk proverbs. Make it catchy. Make it emotionally laden. Make it simple. D. Residual Resolution Emotion (RRE): How do you want the TA to feel? People tend to be deeply affected by the emotional state they are left with after seeing, reading, or hearing something. These are the feelings a person has at the end or climax of a narrative/storyline that motivates, energizes, or even inspires them to do something. E. The check: Will stories based on these themes (Step 2B) adequately address the TA's vulnerabilities, susceptibilities, issues, and sources of pain (Step 1) and therefore be likely to create the outcomes you seek (Step 2A)?
  1. Metaphoric Image Create a single, powerful image in both words and visual imagery that represents the central theme identified in Step 2 for this story and that carries a strong emotional punch.
  1. Context and Relevance Check If your story is not relevant to your TA, they will dismiss the story. Thus, check relevance before actual story development. Be sure you can answer the following:
  • Why should they "buy" this information and "pay" with their time, thought, memory, and attention?
  • How will my information fit in with what they already know & believe?
  • How will they see that your information benefits them?
  • How will they answer the question "What does this have to do with me?"
  1. Real World Constraints Many real-world constraints (time, knowledge, legal constraints, etc.) can act to limit the narrative options available to you. You must live within these constraints. However, your TA will not consider the constraints that affect you. They will consider and judge only the story material you provide.
  1. Identify Key Character Positions This is, for many, the hardest part of purposeful story development. But it need not be. You have three options for these characters (real humans, prototypical but fictional humans, fictional characters—think everything from myths and legends to children's tales to folk tales to cartoons). How to choose? Here are four criteria:
  • Will this character be relevant to, and of interest to, my TA?
  • Can I develop goals, motives, struggles, etc. around this character?
  • Will the story resolution for this character deliver my desired RRE?
  • Can this character deliver my take-away message?

Your audience will use the available story characters to fill four essential character positions. It is important that you begin your story development by defining these four characters and developing them for your TA:

  • Main Character: About whom will it be easiest for you to tell the story?
  • Antagonist Character: Describe the Antagonist so that the TA can clearly visualize and viscerally react to this entity.
  • Climax Character: The Climax Character is the one who needs to act to create a desirable story resolution.
  • Identity Character: Do you want your TA to identify with the Main Character? With the Climax Character? Even with the Antagonist?
  1. Build the story Trust that you have embedded your messaging and use the Eight Essential Elements to build an engaging story for your TA. Keep it as short as you can and still deliver both story and messaging.

A Demonstration of the Proposed Methodology

The greatest value of the concepts presented in this article lie in their use as a guide of the process of story analysis (deconstruction) and development (construction) and, through that process, in the methodical development of more consistently effective narratives.

The following demonstration presents only the final chosen elements that lie behind and form the structure of a plausible story. Yet the guided group development process defined by the information and concepts in this article behind those informational decisions is the real focus of our recommendations, rather than the actual demonstration story elements.

Table II. Demonstration Story Elements

The Situation: Unexploded and uncharted landmines represent one of the greatest current threats in many post-war areas. While a constant threat to all, they are a particularly vicious threat to children. Our assumed purpose for this demonstration is to create a story that will motivate parents – and especially fathers – to actively report these mines.

TA: Rural community fathers who are war veterans

Knowledge Objectives:

  1. One child per day is injured or killed by mines according to local hospital.
  1. 24-hr hotline to report (call or text) landmines is 1-800-BLOWDUP

Belief Objectives:

  1. Reporting landmines to the hotline will save lives.
  1. All the pain of war was for nothing if my family is destroyed.
  1. Only a terrible father would allow his children to be injured or killed.

Desired Outcome: Measurable increases in vigilant detection and reporting of unexploded mines.

Themes:

  • You won the war; don't let an unexploded mine now kill your children.
  • It's no victory if your children are now blown up by an unexploded mine.
  • The responsible man won't allow unexploded mines to now destroy the family he fought a war to save.
  • You can't have the better future you fought for if an unexploded mine now kills your children.

Metaphoric Image: Visual Images to feature: Wailing mothers; weeping fathers carrying injured or dead children through the streets; parents wailing as they search through a playground blown up by a landmine; playing children covered on the screen by a sudden explosion

Audio images to feature: 5 children (ages 5 to 7 years old) singing a well-known children's song and laughing as they play. Background video is of a dirt playground or roadside. With each mine explosion (the sound plays behind the children's voices), one voice goes silent. After the 5th explosion, there is only silence and a black screen.

Table III. Characters

Main Character: The children (heard but not seen in this video story presentation) Goal: Play and live normal lives Antagonist: The mines and (implied) those who buried them Identity Character: We want the TA to identify with the various unnamed wailing, grieving parents included in the video portion of the main body of the presentation. The implied goal of the Identity Character (and, thus, for the TA) is to safeguard their families and – especially – their children. Their implied motive is: as father and head of the family, you are responsible to protect your children from the menace of landmines. It is shameful to fail at this basic duty of fatherhood. Climax Character: The TA; the ones who should do something to protect the Main Character (their children), but who, in this video, haven't done so – and are paying the price with their pain and grief. Resolution: Created to be highly negative. Main Character fails. Climax Character fails. A highly motivational ending. RRE: We want to instill feelings of grief, anger, and guilt that will transmute into a feeling of urgent responsibility. This RRE value should be strong enough to compel immediate action.

Plotline Summary:

  • Opening segment: Black screen. Text: You won the war. Don't let an unexploded mine now kill your children. Fade to: In your region, 1 child each day is injured or killed by a landmine.
  • Body: Sound of 5 young children singing and laughing as they play precedes the first video segment of wailing, anguished parents. First explosion shoots across screen and fades to a moment of black. Only 4 children now singing the song and laughing. Second images of parents wailing over bloodied children. Second explosion. Now only 3 children singing. Continue this pattern to the 5th explosion and silence.
  • Closing Segment: The words roll onto a Black screen: "The responsible man won't allow unexploded mines to now destroy the family he fought a war to save." Fade. Replaced by "Will you be that responsible man?" Fade. Replaced by, "Report unexploded or suspected landmines. Call or text 1-800-BLOWDUP, a 24-hour hotline." Fade to black.

Final Thought

In this paper we present and demonstrate a summary overview of a proposed methodological approach to creating MISO narratives. The methodology is designed to fuse a powerful message that resonates with the intended Target Audience (TA) with an engaging, attention-holding story structure that acts as the delivery vehicle for that messaging. A common missing component in messaging is the effective story structure that engages and holds the attention of the TA, creates personal relevance and context, and causes them to identify, empathize, and adopt the beliefs (subcomponents of attitudes) of the character(s). The methodology we outline in this paper provides that missing component.

The same concepts and tools apply to deconstructing opposing narratives. The strength of our proposed approach stems from its anchor in recent neural science experimental findings (Haven 2013 and 2014) that confirm the informational elements we describe as those that control story engagement, attention, and empathy as well as those that control story influence.

The descriptions we offer in this paper are summary. The paper is intended as a conceptual introduction only. Full details and descriptions are available in various reference documents.
