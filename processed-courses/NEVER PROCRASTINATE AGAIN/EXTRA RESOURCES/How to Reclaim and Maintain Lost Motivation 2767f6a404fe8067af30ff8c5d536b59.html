How to Reclaim and Maintain Lost Motivation.

Do you struggle to stay motivated?

We’ve all been there. We set a goal for ourselves. We get all hyped up to crush it. We start putting in the work… then we lose our motivation, give up, and stop trying .

People are motivated to do something to the degree they believe that something will lead to their desired outcome. Higher belief equals higher motivation.

For example, the more we believe improving our diet and exercise habits will improve our health, the more likely we are to stick to those improvements.

But it also works the other way around. If we don’t think an action will lead to our desired outcome, we’re unlikely to keep doing it . Our motivation drops and we give up before we reach the top of our mountain - or, even worse, never start climbing it at all.

One of the most common reasons we give up on our goal is a lack of results. We put in the work but we don’t always get the results we hoped for.

This lack of results makes us frustrated and makes us doubt that taking those actions and sticking to them will actually lead to achieving the goal.

This is how we go from high belief and high motivation to low belief and low motivation.

So how do we keep our motivation high even if we’re not getting the results we want?

1) Focus on identity, not just outcomes

Many people focus on outcome-based goals like losing 20 pounds or running a 5K. Few consider changing their identity to match those outcomes. As a result, people either don’t achieve their goals or they go back to their old habits immediately after they achieve them.

This is why most participants on the Biggest Loser reality show regained weight when the show ended. If you instead focus on the new identity you want to have and take actions appropriate to that identity, you're more likely to do what you need to do to achieve your desired outcomes and your desired identity.

It's helpful to think about more than the outcomes you want - think about why you want those outcomes. What identities do you want to have?

You don't just want the money. You want to be free.

You don't just want the car. You want to be high status.

You don't just want to lose weight. You want to be healthy and attractive.

Identities can also be "not" something. Not a binge drinker. Not a smoker. Not a complainer.

Figuring out the identities you want to achieve helps you figure out what actions you should be taking to not just achieve those identities but maintain them.

2) Make a plan.

Research has found people who create a plan of when, where, and how they will take goal-directed behavior are much more likely to achieve their goals compared to people who don’t form one.

These plans are known as implementation intentions . Implementation intentions were also found to help subjects overcome self-regulatory problems, avoid bad influences, and recognize and act on opportunities relating to their goal.

An important piece of this concept is “if-then” planning. By that I mean “if situation X occurs, then I will take goal-directed action Y”. When you’re making your plan, consider things that can impede your goal. For example, “if I get invited to have dinner with friends, I will just have a light meal.”

Don’t get into such detail that you get bogged down in planning before you ever begin working towards your goal. Just create enough of a plan to get started and modify the goal as you go along.

3) Ask yourself what happened when things don't go according to plan

What if things don't go according to plan? Ask yourself why. An incredibly underrated technique is asking yourself questions to figure out why things aren’t going as well as they could be.

By that, I literally mean grab a pen and paper, write down “Why did <negative thing> happen?”, and then write down the answers that come to mind.

For example, when I have a week where I wasn’t as healthy as I planned to be, I ask myself what happened, think about what went wrong, and write down the things that happened and what I can do better going forward.

This process of asking yourself questions, thinking about the answers, and planning how to do better next time creates a cycle of reflection, iteration, and improvement. As you can see, this relates back to planning, since you update your plan with new strategies.

Without this iteration, you’ll have a harder time reaching your goals. Why? Because when you hit speed bumps along the way, you won’t be as aware of what happened and what you can do. You’ll take the hit and either keep moving with less information, or you’ll stop moving due to less motivation.

In summary, to keep your motivation high, you want to focus on your desired identity, make a plan to achieve it, and ask yourself what happened when things didn't go according to plan.
