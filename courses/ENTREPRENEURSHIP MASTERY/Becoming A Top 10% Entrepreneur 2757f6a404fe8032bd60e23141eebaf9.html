The Bottom 90% of Entrepreneurs

In this section we will cover the 6 key mistakes of the bottom 90% of entrepreneurs who get subpar results in business.

Mistake #1: They Are Fuelled By The Wrong Desire

There’s two sources of desire that entrepreneurs can tap into:

  1. Personal Gain
  1. Purposeful Mission

Almost all entrepreneurs start their journey with the desire for personal gain. They want to escape the 9-5, get a better lifestyle and make more money. But chasing personal gain makes you a weaker entrepreneur because it leads to the next 5 mistakes…

Mistake #2: They Make Bad Decisions

Since they are only focused on personal gain, the bottom 90% of entrepreneurs go wherever the money seems to be.

When an entrepreneur makes decisions based on the question "What will make me money?" they appear erratic and unfocused, displaying a fickle, almost ADHD-like behaviour. Their heads are on a swivel because they get shiny object syndrome, jumping onto the latest trends and switching business models again and again.

Mistake #3: They Don’t Prioritise The Customer

The bottom 90% of entrepreneurs often make poor products that fail to deliver the promised results because they are more focused on getting sales for personal gain rather than helping the customer.

As a result, they constantly need new customers because their existing customers don’t retain.

Since their products provide poor results, these entrepreneurs never feel fulfilled by the impact their business is having on the world. Their satisfaction comes solely from the cha-ching of new sales.

Mistake #4: They Don’t Care About Personal Fulfilment

They work hard every day, but they don’t feel fulfilled with their work. That's because they don’t care about the work they are doing other than as a means to make money. They spend all day on tasks that aren't enjoyable, serving people they don't care about, just for the sake of earning money.

Sounds like a 9-5 job, doesn’t it?

These bottom 90% entrepreneurs often become more materialistic, suffering from lifestyle inflation. They trade their life satisfaction for money, so naturally, they spend more on shiny toys to try to fill the void left by a lack of a purposeful mission.

Mistake #5: They Are Hoping For Completion

Since they are unfulfilled, they don’t want to live like this forever. They make the common mistake of hoping for a completion of this chapter in their lives. They tell themselves:

“I’ll just make the money now, and then I can change things later.”

This is how men lose decades of their lives. It's the great fallacy to think that someday in the future you’ll start working on your purposeful mission. Any day not spent working on your purposeful mission is a day wasted, even if you saved up money.

They are essentially hoping for retirement, except they have 10 times the stress and responsibility of 9-5 workers since they are business owners. These hard working men, who had so much potential, reduce themselves to being mules, sacrificing their days just to work for money that they spend trying to fill a void that only a purposeful mission can fill.

Mistake #6: They Have Poor Impulse Control

Even if the bottom 90% of entrepreneurs make good money, they don't keep it because of poor impulse control.

At the start of the journey, distractions are basic, like porn and video games. Resist these, and you get to the next level. But once you've achieved a great level of success, suddenly you have beautiful women who want to be with you, and you're invited to exciting parties and collaborations. Can you still resist the temptation when it's not just porn but a 7/10 girl who wants you to fuck her without a condom?

This is how giants fall. Athletes, entrepreneurs, and companies reach success, but if they were motivated by personal gain, they get too distracted when the rewards of success appear. Ego creeps in, and they believe they no longer need to grind as hard as they used to. Complacency sets in, and they are overtaken by competitors who remain purpose-driven.

Realistic Examples of The Bottom 90% of Entrepreneurs

  • Online Coaches Who Focus on Sales, Not Client Results: These entrepreneurs are more concerned with closing deals and increasing their revenue than ensuring their clients achieve real results. Their primary motivation is getting the next sale, not the success of their clients.
  • Self-Help YouTubers Who Optimise for Views, Not Helping Viewers: These YouTubers create shallow content to try to boost their view counts, rather than providing genuine, helpful advice.
  • Wantrepreneur Switching Business Model to Business Model: These beginners constantly chase the latest business models they hear about, switching as soon as the work gets hard. They lack long-term vision and commitment, resulting in a lack of depth in any single business and ultimately leading to burnout and frustration.

  1. Can you think of times you acted like a bottom 90% entrepreneur? Why did you behave like that? What emotion, vision and desire caused it? You might want to stay aware and not allow those emotions to drive you into more actions in the future.

The Top 10% of Entrepreneurs

In this section we will cover the 5 strategies of the top 10% of entrepreneurs who get the best results in business.

Strategy #1: Start With The Right Desire

The top 10% of entrepreneurs often start their journey desiring personal gain, just like the bottom 90%. The difference is that the top entrepreneurs eventually realise the better way to play the game of business - with purpose and the intention to help others.

Imagine your business as a car and money as the fuel that powers it. Just as a car isn't designed to sit idle with a full tank of gas, a business isn't meant to exist solely to make money. The true purpose of a car is to take you places, using its fuel to reach meaningful destinations. Similarly, the true purpose of a business is to use the money it generates to drive change, and make a positive impact in the world. Money is simply the means to an end, not the end itself.

“ What’s the purpose of your business? ”

90% entrepreneurs “ To make me money ” 10% entrepreneurs “ To make an impact ”

The top 10% of entrepreneurs start by figuring out what "destination" they want to reach or what impact they want their business to create. They identify a purposeful mission that means a lot to them.

Why is it more powerful to think about a mission instead of just being focused on making money?

If your mission is about helping others, you’ll get more support from customers. When you operate your business with a purposeful mission, people want to support you because they are inspired by your mission, which they feel will help them.

“He who has a why to live for can bear almost any how”

Friedrich Nietzsche

Compare that to the bottom 90% of entrepreneurs who only think about the money they can make for themselves. Who would want to support that mission? Who gives a fuck that some random entrepreneur wants to make money for himself? What can he do for us?

In the era of social media, you need people to be on your side and to believe in your vision. If your vision is just about you making money, people won’t support it. If it’s a genuine mission to help the world, people will literally help you for free, support you, buy your products, comment, like, and share, leading to exponential growth.

When people are inspired by the vision and mission of a company, they buy not just once but repeatedly, until the company loses vision.

How do you ensure you don’t lose vision? By playing the infinite game…

You need to start as you mean to go on. Instead of creating a business like the 90% who aim to 'complete' it once they have enough money and then pursue the mission they truly care about, start with the mission from the beginning!

It's like marrying a woman you don't find attractive, knowing you'll divorce her eventually when you level up. This approach only leads to resentment over the years you’re together, and when you finally 'complete' it, you're back to square one. It’s better to start with the right mission from the beginning.

How do you find the right mission for you?

Start by choosing the customer you genuinely want to help. Instead of chasing where you think the most money is, think about the kind of person you actually want to spend your time helping. Think about the pain they are experiencing due to a problem you can solve.

“Who should I help with my business?”

Often, the best answer is simply your younger self. You have empathy and understanding for people who are experiencing the same pains you did when you were younger. You can speak their language. Most importantly, you know what they need to do to get to where you are today.

Once you are clear on who you want to help, you need to decide what to help them with. What specific pain will you solve in their lives? To answer this, think about your most frustrating days. What caused that pain, and how did you solve it?

If it was very frustrating for you, it's likely very frustrating for people like your younger self. People pay good money to get rid of frustration. If you become exceptional at solving that niche problem, your ideal customers will flock to you.

Write out a clear goal statement that encapsulates the problem you aim to help your customers with:

I will help 10,000 teenage boys half their depression scores by the end of 2026

I will get 1 million young men on self improvement by the end of 2026

I will help 200 young men get compatible girlfriends by the end of 2026

This is your mission.

By starting with a purposeful mission, you build the foundations of your business from the ground up, getting clear on the mission you want to dedicate years of your life to. To fulfil your mission, you need to move on to the second strategy of the top 10% entrepreneurs...

Strategy #2: Prioritise The Customer

The top 10% of entrepreneurs are customer obsessed.

They start by taking extreme ownership of customer results, knowing they are fully responsible for delivering the promised outcomes. They strive to ensure their product or service delivers what was promised so that the customer is genuinely satisfied.

For example, when a customer buys a fat loss program, the top 10% entrepreneur does everything possible to help the customer lose the fat. This approach, which seems like common sense, is what you'd expect from a committed entrepreneur. However, the bottom 90% of entrepreneurs are often just glad they made the sale and immediately shift their focus to the next sale, neglecting to ensure the customer actually loses the fat.

Most of the time, the customer does not achieve the promised result. This is particularly true with do-it-yourself info products, which are popular today. Many customers buy an online course and don’t even finish watching the modules, let alone take action. The bottom 90% of entrepreneurs think the customer is at fault, but that belief doesn’t help them improve. The top 10% of entrepreneurs take responsibility and strategize on how to improve customer results. Maybe they need to make the course more captivating with different video formats or editing, or perhaps they need to offer accountability to ensure customers actually complete the course and take action. Whatever the solution, the best entrepreneurs are those who take responsibility for their customers and creatively come up with ways to get their customers better results through their product.

Attracting higher-quality customers is another way to increase overall customer success rates. By setting stricter standards for who they sell to, top entrepreneurs can focus on individuals who are more likely to benefit from their product or service. This selective approach allows them to dedicate their resources and support to those committed to success, rather than trying to sell to everyone indiscriminately.

Consider the difference between customers who join a program marketed as “Make $10k/Month Fast” versus “Learn The Skills to Become a Better Entrepreneur”. It could be the same program on the inside, just marketed differently, attracting vastly different customers. By marketing honestly and setting realistic expectations, top entrepreneurs draw in customers who are serious about putting in the work and achieving meaningful results.

When focused on customer results, entrepreneurs start to identify holes in their product and can work on improving each area. They analyse whether customers are using their product correctly, if new instructions are needed, if parts of the product are ineffective or outdated, and constantly strive to improve the product.

They track and measure metrics for customer results, using questionnaires and feedback forms to assess the progress their customers have made after purchasing their product or service. Having a clear view of this data shows top entrepreneurs how effective their product truly is and provides ideas for improvement. They publish this data for their marketing and let it speak for itself.

Sometimes prioritising the customer means sacrificing money and other resources to ensure better results for them, especially in the short term. Many giant companies operated at a loss for years, ruthlessly reinvesting back into the business, into software developers, and other team members to build a better product for the customer.

Along with this, the top 10% of entrepreneurs spend more money on their product. They reinvest revenue back into improving the product, hiring employees to better care for customers, and investing in software and training to enhance the customer experience. Intelligent reinvestment into the business is a key skill of top entrepreneurs.

Imagine if you could put $10k in and get $100k out.

By making decisions that prioritise the customer, top entrepreneurs improve the results their customers get from their product. This leads to happier customers, more referrals, positive word-of-mouth marketing, and the entrepreneur having more conviction in their product, making it easier to sell. All of this means these entrepreneurs often go on to make more money in the long term.

Strategy #3: Go All In

Top 10% entrepreneurs are not just involved in their businesses; they are fully committed and willing to align all aspects of their lives to their mission. This means dedicating significant periods with complete focus on their business, often sacrificing leisure and comfort to ensure their goals are met.

Why not enjoy more free time and ‘fun’, instead of grinding so hard?

When man can't find meaning in his life, he distracts himself with pleasure -

Viktor Frankl

When a person doesn’t go all in on a mission that gives them meaning, they end up with too much free time. What do you think a man without a purposeful mission does in that free time?

Degeneracy. Porn. Video Games. Junk Food. Content Consumption.

The easiest thing to fill up free time with is often degenerate bad habits that do not serve you. Often, this isn’t even intentional—degeneracy just finds its way in.

Imagine a glass container with rocks and sand. The rocks represent the essential things in your life, like working on your mission, maintaining your health, and nurturing relationships. The sand represents the degenerate bad habits.

If we don’t put the rocks in first, the sand fills up the container and leaves less space for the rocks. The best life is one where we have filled our container of time with as many rocks as possible and then do our best to plug the holes to prevent degeneracy from seeping in.

Of course, top entrepreneurs dedicate time to exercise, family, and self improvement, but they avoid leaving so much free time that it can be filled with degenerate bad habits like porn, video games, and excessive content consumption, which the bottom 90% often engage in.

“Idle hands are the devil’s workshop”

By dedicating more of their free time to their mission, top entrepreneurs not only make more progress in their business but also leave less time for bad habits.

Another reason why going all in on your mission is so important is to prevent mind wandering.

A wandering mind is an unhappy mind…

People spend 46.9 percent of their waking hours thinking about something other than what they’re doing, and this mind-wandering typically makes them unhappy.

Mind wandering is when you are not present and instead are lost in your thoughts, inner dialogue, memories, and fantasies.

When you are mind wandering, you are always less happy than if you were present, even if your thoughts are positive or the activity you're engaged in is unenjoyable! The graph on the right shows a happiness score as it correlates to being present (no inner dialogue) vs mind wandering (thinking).

Basically, being in deep thought will always make you less happy than if you were present.

This doesn’t mean that you never want your mind to wander—sometimes it can be great for creativity and spontaneous ideas. However, allowing your mind to wander should be done intentionally. Most people experience unintentional mind wandering all the time, even during important life experiences like speaking to loved ones and working on their mission.

There is a key to reducing mind wandering that all top 10% entrepreneurs have experienced...

Flow.

Flow is a state where you are immersed in an activity, fully present, with no mind wandering—what athletes call being 'in the zone.' When you enter a flow state, it's like you become 'one with the task.' Your inner dialogue shuts off, and you enter a positive feeling state, allowing you to get the work done to the best of your ability.

The top 10% of entrepreneurs do whatever it takes to tap into a flow state more often because it increases concentration, resulting in higher quality work. It gives them clarity, so they know what to do, results in positive emotions that feel good, stops mind wandering and self-consciousness (which are usually negative experiences), and, most importantly, makes the work more enjoyable.

How much would you pay for those benefits for life? It could be worth millions of $ to be able to tap into a flow state more often. Huge leverage!

Flow happens in activities that are the perfect level of challenge for your skills.

If the challenge of an activity is too difficult for your skills, it puts you in the 'Anxiety' section of the following diagram. It leads to fearful, negative emotions. For example, joining a martial arts gym and having to spar with their best fighter who doesn’t go easy on you. It’s like trying to defeat a boss in a video game that you have no chance against—the challenge is too hard and it gets frustrating.

If the challenge of an activity is too easy for your skills, it puts you in the 'Boredom' section of the diagram. For instance, sparring with a beginner who doesn’t know how to fight. Winning easily becomes boring quickly. It’s like grinding low-level monsters in a video game that you can defeat in one shot—there’s no challenge.

But when the challenge of an activity is perfect for your skills, meaning it's not too hard or too easy, you are likely to enter a flow state. For example, sparring with someone who joined the martial arts gym six months ago, just like you. Both of you have nearly equal skill levels and are going hard enough to require focus, but not so hard that you fear serious injury. You take hits, maybe almost lose, but it requires all your effort to win. If you had this fight 10 times, you might lose five of them.

Engaging in activities that place you in this perfect zone of challenge stretches your skills and takes you to the next level, giving you a positive sense of progression.

Strategy #4: Essentialism

In your journey to becoming a top 10% entrepreneur, one of the most transformative shifts you can make is becoming an Essentialist. In this section, you’ll learn how using 'brain points' correctly is the key to success and how to use essentialism and monk mode to achieve it.

Imagine your mental energy as a finite currency called "brain points." Each day, you start with a limited number of brain points, and how you allocate them determines your success in life.

Every action you take throughout the day costs brain points. Checking your phone first thing in the morning might seem harmless, but it can cost you 20 brain points right away. These seemingly small tasks add up quickly, leading to 'Death by 1000 Cuts,' where your potential is sapped not by significant events, but by hundreds of 'invisible' mistakes throughout the day. Most people live their lives this way, with their brain points being drained from all angles without even realising it.

When it comes time to do deep, mentally demanding work, like recording content, you might find yourself feeling incapable of even beginning because you don’t have enough brain points left. A big task might cost, say, 200 brain points, but if you’ve already spent your brain points on deciding what to eat multiple times a day, replying to messages and other minor tasks, you’ll be below the 200 required for the big deep work task. As a result, you’ll feel overwhelmed, procrastinate, or simply lack the energy to perform at your best.

Stuff that uses brain points:

  • Relationship drama
  • Useless group chats
  • Useless content consumption
  • Reading comments
  • Checking notifications
  • Internet drama and beef
  • Video games
  • Brain fog-inducing food
  • Going to school or work
  • Gym
  • Making decisions like what to eat or wear

Trying to work on your business after going to school or work, hitting the gym, replying to everyone on your phone and then eating is absurd!

If your mission is the priority, you should conserve your brain points for it and do it first thing in the morning, even if you have to get up earlier.

By the time the bottom 90% of entrepreneurs sit down to work, they have already spent most of their brain points throughout the day, leaving them wondering why they almost never feel mentally capable of tackling the work they’ve been procrastinating on for a while.

Top 10% entrepreneurs conserve their brain points for their mission by ruthlessly sacrificing through essentialism.

Essentialists do fewer things, but better. Instead of trying to make progress in many different areas of life like the average person does, an Essentialist discerns what truly matters and dedicates their time and energy to those priorities.

They eliminate non-essential tasks, focusing their brain points on activities that align with their core mission.

Essentialists choose a few big goals that mean a lot to them and sacrifice anything that isn’t a part of those goals, accepting the trade off.

One way Essentialists organise periods of their life is through monk mode. Monk mode is a short period of intense isolation from distractions, with complete focus on the mission.

Top entrepreneurs regularly enter periods of monk mode, setting aside specific blocks of time where all distractions are minimised or eliminated. They turn off notifications, avoid social media, and set clear boundaries to ensure uninterrupted work.

Monk mode works because reaching success requires dedicating 80% of your total daily brain points to your essential tasks. In a normal, balanced life, too many brain points are spent on random things, preventing significant breakthroughs.

Success often requires periods of imbalance where you go all in.

Usually, you can’t live like this forever. Eventually, you’ll want to spend more time with friends, family, and dating, rather than just grinding in front of a computer screen. Therefore, it makes sense to alternate intense periods of monk mode, where you focus entirely on your mission, with periods of 'fun mode,' where you unwind, do what you enjoy, and spend time with people to make sure you don’t lose your social skills.

You will undeniably create more success by alternating periods of extreme focus with periods of extreme fun than by living a balanced life with a bit of work and a bit of fun every day.

Strategy #5: Preparing For The Fall

Getting success is only half the battle; KEEPING the success is harder. The top 10% of entrepreneurs know that complacency, unforeseen challenges, and mistakes can cause them to lose what they have built. Learning why the giants who were once at the top of their industry fell off can help you avoid the same mistakes.

When you build success, you’ll encounter the ‘woman in the red dress.’ This refers to the increasingly sexy distractions that present themselves as you climb up and pull you away from the essentialist life you were living before.

You started by completely dedicating your brain points to your business, optimising every minute of your day, and ruthlessly subtracting people and even hobbies you liked so you could go all in on business.

And you achieve the success you wanted for so long.

With success comes distractions like other people who want a piece of you: men who want to befriend you, women who want to be with you, and companies that want to sponsor you. Suddenly, you’re spending 100 brain points a week checking emails from potential sponsors and using decision-making power that you should reserve for your business on their offers. You’re getting more matches on dating apps and having fun conversations with attractive women, spending 30 minutes on FaceTime when you used to just read or go to bed early.

Today's results come from last year's work.

All these distractions seem insignificant, even pleasant, but you weren’t engaging in them when you were building your success. You broke through to success by dedicating as many brain points as possible to your mission, and now you’re riding the wave of that momentum. It feels like you are the special one who can now take time off and enjoy the distractions without losing your success. It’s easy to cope and believe these distractions will even bring you more success…

“I’ve got to go to parties because then I can get more content for my social media”

It’s the YouTuber who pops off on the algorithm and starts going on podcasts and doing collaborations, distracting themselves from what made them successful in the first place—focusing on making THEIR videos.

It’s the MMA fighter who climbs the ranks and becomes everyone's favourite but then parties too hard, starts taking drugs, stays up late, and misses training sessions because they think they’re the best, only to get knocked out by a competitor who hasn’t missed a session in years.

Now that you’ve built the success, you’re less hungry.

Empty balls & a full stomach kill a man's ambitions.

On the climb up you’re dissatisfied with life, you’re aggressive and arrogant, clawing at success each and every day. You’re hungry and you’re pissed off that you don’t have what you want. So you obsess over it. You go above and beyond.

But when you’ve gotten used to the success, it’s just normal life now. It’s not even that special. Life goes on. You realise there’s probably more important things to focus on, like happiness and love and balance. You have the money, fame and status anyway so now you can afford to take your eyes off the ball.

So you eat like a king and fuck like a caveman until you’re nice and relaxed. All the while there’s a killer who’s grinding just like you were on your come up, and he has a target on your head.

At this point, it's easy for the winner to become egotistical and think they don’t need to train as hard, do that extra session, or maintain their disciplined habits. They believe they can stay up a little later and relax on their routines because they are still amazing at their craft. But very slowly, they start getting worse. It's just cause and effect. They slack on the things that caused their success in the first place.

Then comes their first loss—the first domino knocking down the great empire they’ve built.

They’re frustrated “I don’t lose!”

Maybe they get their act together and return to the essentialist life they were living on the come-up, or maybe they don’t. Either way, it’s too late. They’re on a losing streak, their self-image is deteriorating, and their fans are leaving. They can never return to the undefeated glory they once held.

If you want to keep climbing, you have to remain undistracted by the woman in the red dress.

It's okay to slow down in business to prioritise other goals like family and health, but don’t expect to stay at the same level in your business when your competitors are fully focused. If you’re willing to make that tradeoff, go ahead and enjoy the balanced lifestyle. If not, don’t delude yourself into thinking that being distracted is productive. You will always make more progress in your mission when you dedicate as many of your brain points to it as possible.

What do the top 10% of entrepreneurs do to protect themselves from falling off?

Check The Ego: Top entrepreneurs understand the importance of humility and self-awareness. They have a trusted inner circle who are not afraid to call them out when necessary, avoiding the trap of surrounding themselves with ‘Yes-Men’. Additionally, they continually seek to learn and experiment for better results, avoiding the mindset of “this is how we’ve always done it.” By remaining open to new ideas and approaches, they prevent complacency.

Foresee The Failure: Successful entrepreneurs proactively think about potential distractions and failures that could arise. They use humility to think to themselves “what could take me off course?” By anticipating these challenges, they can be ready to make the right choice.

Resist The Distraction: Training willpower and discipline is crucial for top entrepreneurs. Not only do they continue to get the hard work done, they resist the distractions by practising self-control. It’s not easy, it’s a continuous decision they may have to make many times every single day.

The top 10% do, even though they are less focused on the money! They receive more support from people because of their wholesome, customer-focused mission. They think long-term because they actually find fulfilment in their lives right now, which motivates them to work harder and focus on making a better product for the customer.

Becoming A Top 10% Entrepreneur (ACTION STEPS)

Now that we’ve fully covered the differences between the top 10% of entrepreneurs and the bottom 90%, it’s time to go over the practical action steps you can take right now to get into the top 10%.

Action Step 1: Tap Into The Right Desire

Start at the foundation by choosing the mission you want to embark on, not just the money you want to make.

How to Turn On the Desire for a Purposeful Mission:

Awareness : Understand that prioritising a purposeful mission will lead to a better outcome (most people are unaware of this). You've just learned this today.

Action Step 2: Discipline Yourself to Prioritise the Customer

Prioritising the customer is not a one-time decision; it’s a daily discipline.

You have to constantly meditate back to this intention with all of the micro-decisions you make in business.

Copy the decision-making framework below and answer the questions before making a business decision. You can place the questions in a note page on your phone or computer, and then copy them as a template every time you’re about to make a decision.

Decision Making Framework:

  1. What is the problem/symptom I’m experiencing?
  1. What outcome do I want?
  1. What would a wise top 10% entrepreneur do to solve the problem and reach the outcome?
  1. Are there alternative options that I’m not considering?
  1. What decision would best prioritise customer results?
  1. Will I suffer in some way if I choose the decision that best serves the customer?
  1. How can I mitigate this suffering so I can prioritise the customer and reduce the cost to me?
  1. Decision Made: I will…

After making a decision with this framework, report back 2 weeks later and summarise the results and lessons learned.

Action Step 4: Conserve Your Brain Points

Take these 5 steps to conserve your brain points for your business work.

  1. Analyse Your Pre-Business Work Routine :
    • Think of every micro task: getting ready, brushing teeth, checking your phone, making food, eating, commuting, school, gym, etc.
  1. Destroy the ‘Any Benefit’ Mindset :
    • Just because an activity has some benefit doesn’t mean you should do it. It might cost more than it gives.
    • For example, spending 15 minutes reading Twitter in the morning might seem beneficial, but it probably distracts you more than it helps.
  1. Subtract the Biggest Sinks :
    • Identify the biggest sinks, the most tiresome, those that make you overthink, the most physical, and those that make you emotional.
    • Eliminate or at least move the brain point sinks till after your business work.
  1. Change Your Routine :
    • Plan Ahead: Go to your calendar and plan a better routine.
    • Work Early: Consider waking up early to work on your business for 4 hours before school or work, even if it means you have to go to sleep at 7-8pm. Get it done, if business is the priority.
    • Limit Distractions: Leave your phone in a different room, turn off notifications, or use airplane mode.
    • Set Reminders: Go on your phone and set an alarm to remind you of this change, preventing you from slipping into autopilot mode.
    • Create a Checklist: Write a checklist for tomorrow and keep it open on your computer screen and phone so it’s the first thing you see when you wake up.
  1. Iterate and Improve :
    • Continuously refine your routine to avoid brain point loss before work.

Action Step 5: Prepare For Your Fall

Have the humility to accept that there are reasons why you might get distracted and fall off.

  1. Journal This Question : What could be the most realistic reason why you fall off?
  1. Foresee Failure : Visualise it happening and imagine yourself rejecting it.
  1. Practise Mental Rehearsal : Close your eyes, see the potential distraction arise (e.g., a girl you’ve just met who wants you fuck her without a condom), and visualize yourself doing the right thing to keep yourself on track.
  1. Speak Honestly With Your Inner Circle: Tell your family, friends and team to keep you in check and to help you watch out for the kind of distractions that you are most likely to fall for.
