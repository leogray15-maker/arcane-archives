How to Learn Like A Nobel Prize Winner.

The week is young. What will you do with yours?

If you haven't made plans yet, I suggest you spend some of it learning something.

The act of learning expands and sharpens your mind. Learning one thing makes it easier for your brain to learn something else. This creates something like compound interest.

There's a powerful technique that will help you quickly reach the 1% in whatever topic you choose. See, Nobel Prize winning physicist Richard Feynman had a way to study more effectively than any of his peers. It's a four-step process known as the Feynman Technique .

Step 1) Teach it to a Child

Imagine you're preparing to explain what you know about your chosen topic to a 12-year-old. You'd have to use simple words and concepts, right? That's exactly the point.

As Albert Einstein (another pretty smart dude) said - if you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough. This isn't true in every case , as some concepts are far too complex to explain simply. However, the vast majority of topics can be explained simply by distilling the key concepts and connections.

But for you to be able to explain something simply, you must first understand it deeply. If you have to rely on complex jargon and technical terms, you simply don't understand it well enough yet.

Grab a pen and paper and write down what you'd want to teach that 12-year-old. Remember, you have to explain it in simple words and concepts. As you write, you'll notice places where you get stuck.

Sometimes it'll be because you feel like you can't simplify any further. Other times it'll be because you have a hole in your knowledge in that area. Take note of these places and come back to them when you've dumped everything you can out of your brain.

Step 2) Review

Now imagine you're the 12-year-old. Grab a juice box and start reading what you wrote. Picture yourself as a child who has absolutely no previous knowledge about this topic.

As you read, do you understand what you're reading? If you do - great! You know your stuff. But if you find parts you don't understand (or you couldn't simplify as you wrote), then you have work to do. Note these places.

Refer to your source material - books, videos, podcasts, and so on. Learn more about the places you couldn't explain simply - or explain at all. Update what you wrote, then read through it again. Repeat this process until your inner 12-year-old understands everything on the page(s).

Step 3) Organize Your Notes

Chances are, you're going to have to repeat Step 2 a few times. I know I often do...

This means you're going to be left with a mess of scribbles. Now, take those scribbles and organize them - neatly write them onto fresh pages, or type them into your favorite program. This will enable you to easily refer to your simplified notes later on. Better yet, it'll enable you to...

Step 4) Teach Someone Else

One of the best feelings in the world is teaching someone something. That's one of the things I love most about being a coach and a creator.

Find a person you can teach about your subject. Since you simplified your notes, they won’t need to have prior knowledge of the topic. All they need is their teacher (you), and depending on their age, a frosty juice box or a beer.
