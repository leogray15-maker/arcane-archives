Busting 3 Productivity Myths.

Have you ever wanted to be more productive? You're not alone. We're constantly looking for ways to get more done in less time.

Unfortunately for us, that search often leads us to ineffective, or worse, harmful advice. There are many myths about how to become more productive - myths that actually harm your productivity.

Let's bust three of the worst myths...

1) "You have to be busy to be productive"

We've all seen those silly "motivational" images. You know, the ones with guys wearing suits and Rolexes with quotes like I'LL SLEEP WHEN I'M DEAD. Or even better, the ones with lions wearing crowns and jewelry.

Why is a lion wearing a crown? How did he put it on? So many questions...

Some people seem to actually believe that you should work as much as possible and sleep as little as possible. They think the number of hours they work is something you should brag about. "Dude, I've been so busy lately! Working 12 hour days every day!" That's nothing to be proud of.

Truth is, you don't have to grind all day (and all night) to be productive. 2 hours of focused deep work will get you better results than 12 hours of being "busy" but unfocused.

2) "Multitasking makes you more productive"

Multitasking - focusing on more than one thing at a time - isn't something the human brain is actually capable of. The brain isn't like the multi-core multi-threaded processor in your laptop. It can't focus on multiple things simultaneously.

note: yes, you can do multiple basic things at one time like talk and chew gum... but here we're discussing the idea of productively doing multiple work tasks at one time.

The brain is more like an older single-core single-thread processor. It seems like it's running multiple programs. In reality, it's context switching from program A to program B back to program A. The context switches in your brain happen too fast for you to perceive, so it seems like you're multitasking.

But you're not. And instead of your attempt at "multitasking" helping your performance, it actually hurts your performance. Why? Because each of those context switches costs time and energy. That's why you feel so exhausted after a busy day of trying to get everything done at once.

3) "Breaks make you less productive"

Imagine you're at the gym. You pick heavy things up. You put them back down. Rinse and repeat. You're working to build muscle.

But are you going to do a set of 50 max-weight squats back to back with no rest? Of course not, right? That'd be silly! You'd injure yourself. You could be out for weeks. You'd lose some gains. You'd never get to talk to that cute girl who inexplicably wears glasses while running.

Mental and physical performance have many parallels. One of those parallels is that your brain needs breaks just like your body does. Otherwise, you'll burn yourself out.

Every hour or so, take a break for a few minutes. Based on what I've read, the sweet spot for optimal performance is around 50 minutes of work followed by a 10 minute break.

Take breaks, or you will break.
