Transparency

Transparency is disarming.

If you go back in time during Trump and Romney's presidential, you'll realise Trump was transparent about him being rich

Whereas Romney wasn't.

Romney thought it was a negative being rich and once he was found out about being rich, that's when it became a negative.

Do you understand?

You didn't know he had 15 houses until the New York Times did a big story with pictures of all 15 houses.

You didn't know he had an underground eight-car garage with an elevator until somebody did a story about that.

Trump from the very beginning was transparent about all of this stuff. Even in one of his speeches, he said "I'm really, really rich."

Romney put himself in a box where every time somebody wanted to hit him they just hit him with the fact that you're rich and that, therefore, is bad.

There's no way to hit Trump with this.

It's good.

A lot of the thinking in those times were run by this if you were found out to be rich and were hiding it:

"This prick is rich and I'm not,"

Every time he tried to cover it, he made it worse.

The turning point of the election was when Romney was apologizing for this thing he's getting caught at - being rich.

People think, "Oh I don't want people to know that about me."

No, you want them to know all about you because everything you've got is some sort of rapport bridge for somebody and by the very act of disclosure, you disarm people's defences.

Remember FORM.

People hear these things by the way almost as dog whistles. You want to remember that too.

I'll give you a couple of real life examples.

There was a speech done a long time ago, I don't remember when, but during the speech, he talked about being bankrupt and his two cars got repossessed.

Hardly anybody hears it except somebody who's been bankrupt or is going through bankruptcy. They hear it loud and clear.

Here's how I know, because they're in the crowd waiting for the speaker to tell him about their bankruptcy story. They heard it and the speaker instantly had cred with them that he wouldn't have had otherwise.

There's a line in the same speech about stuttering so badly as a child, but most people didn't hear that.

But, as disclosure to somebody who's got a kid that stutters or who stutters themselves, and here's how I know because they're waiting to tell them their story with a bag in their hand of stuff they bought after their speech.

These disclosures work, they don't hurt. In many cases, they're not really paid attention to, by people that they don't matter to. But to people that they matter to they build a rapport.
