How To Write Stories That Pack A Punch

The single best author you need to study that will allow you to write stories that pack a punch (Without becoming the next Stephen king or reading endless fiction, plus examples shown).

I didn't learn how to write irresistible copy by just reading copy books or studying ad copy. I learned how to write irresistible copy by combining what I learned inside copy books with the works of Charles Bukowski.

Who is Charles Bukowski?

A simple man who loved three things:

  1. Writing
  1. Women
  1. Booze

Anyhow, reading his work and handwriting in in my own writing is what I credit for giving me a huge leg up on the copy competition.

Most copywriters, you see, study the hardcore direct response stuff (which is amazing), but that’s all they do. As a result, their copy lacks real heart and comes across as cold and hypey. They can’t tell stories, pull on the heartstrings of the target, and make people feel a way.

This greatly limits the pull their copy has.

Not only that, but their copy is usually long winded, not clear, and definitely not irresistible to read.

I know because I used to suffer from these problems myself and it was the works of Charles Bukowski that helped me more than any copywriting book (or copywriter) ever did.

Before we get into it, read this snippet by Charles Bukowski from the book 'Women':

“I was 50 years old and hadn’t been to bed with a woman for four years. I had no women friends. I looked at them as I passed them on the streets or wherever I saw them, but I looked at them without yearning and with a sense of futility. I masturbated regularly, but the idea of having a relationship with a woman - even on non-sexual terms - was beyond my imagination. I had a 6 year old daughter born out of wedlock. She lived with her mother and I paid child support. I had been married years before at the age of 35. That marriage lasted two and one half years. My wife divorced me. I had been in love only once. She had died of acute alcoholism. She died at 48 when I was 38. My wife has been 12 years younger than I. I believe that she too is dead now, although I’m not sure. She wrote me a long letter each Christmas for 6 years after the divorce. I never responded.”

Do you notice how irresistible that is? It’s so clean, simple, and draws you in like honey does to bees.

It makes you want to read. It makes you feel a way.

If only you could write simply and cleanly like that and then combine it with all the direct response knowledge you've learnt.

Now the question is:

How do we do it?

Well, at the time I studied Bukowski like crazy and made a simple checklist for myself called the ‘Bukowski Effect’ that I aimed to follow as best as I could (each day I’d handwrite the works of Charles Bukowski in my own writing. This is all I did for a very long time because I wanted to deeply internalize it. In addition, I’d also practice writing by using my copy knowledge + the Bukowski Effect checklist).

And over time?

I found my writing becoming cleaner and simpler. It carried more punch, pulled on heartstrings, and got the people going.

This, in return, allowed me to write irresistible cold emails, direct mail pieces, sales letters, and emails.

But what exactly was on the Bukowski Effect checklist? A list of reminders/notes/takeaways from my analysis of Bukowski’s writing style that would help enliven my copy, make it irresistible, and hit hard.

Here's the Bukowski Effect Checklist:

  • Start copy with a short irresistible one liner that sucks people in - Don’t waste time setting the scene. Get right to it. Your first one liner should be straight to the point and peak curiosity (i.e. “It began as a mistake.”)
  • Keep a strong pace with your writing. Make it flow with quickness - People need to feel the writing is taking them somewhere. If the writing lacks a strong pace, people get bored. But if the writing is moving, then it becomes like a mini roller coaster and gets the people going. Know this and keep your writing moving forward and don’t let it stop. The moment it stops, the flame dies and people stop reading. Keep the flame alive. Always. (Edit your writing to see where the pace dies. Then fix it.)
  • Avoid dressing up your writing. Keep it naked. Strip away all the layers of clothing. And make it easy on the eyes - It should be as vulnerable as a naked body. If it has too much clothing, then it cannot be felt. Take off all the clothing. Leave only the bare skin. And let this raw contact cut through the reader’s mind and enter his heart.
  • Inject life into your writing - If it doesn’t make you think, understand or feel, then it’s pointless. Lifeless. Get rid of it. And start again.
    • “When you write… each line must be full of a delicious little juice. Flavor. They must be full of power. They must make you want to turn a page… Each line must be an entity to itself.” ~ Bukowski
  • Write with the goal of capturing the raw essence of what it means to be human - This is what I like about Bukowski’s writing. His stories make you feel the rawness of the human experience. Nothing is sugar coated. Everything is told as it is. This increases connection and facilitates understanding. Note: I apply this all the time when I talk about my reader’s BIG problem in my copy. The more raw those sections are, the more they hit.
  • Don’t write to impress, write to communicate clearly (Your writing should be VERY EASY to SKIM) - Even more still, it should easily be understood by people from all walks of life even people who aren’t very fluent in English.
    • This is the test I’d apply to my copy all the time. “Could this be understood by people who know a little bit of English, but not a lot?” If not, make it simpler. The simpler, the better. Keep it very simple. And don’t try to impress. Just write it like it is. When you write to impress, your writing carries an undertone that makes it lack credibility. People can feel this.
  • Write stories like you’re seated around a campfire, sharing your experiences with people who are hanging onto every word. In other words, write like you speak. The more conversational, the more irresistible because it feels like you’re having a real conversation with the reader. This is huge. It’s easy for people to stop reading when the words don’t interest them. It’s harder for people to leave a conversation. The more conversational your writing, the more your copy draws people in. All of Bukowski's work is very conversational. It doesn’t even feel like you’re reading a book. It feels like you’re seated at the bar while you’re hearing stories from a wise old man. Create the same effect with your copy. Make your copy so conversational that people forget they’re reading words on a screen.
  • Be completely honest with your writing - Admit what you don’t want to admit. Admit what makes you look bad. Admit what paints you in a bad light. Keep it real and value direct honesty above all else. People have a deep innate desire to value authenticity and want to know the truth. The more honest your writing, the more you tap into this intrinsic curiosity and hook people. Plus, this makes your reader feel understood because they probably have the same dark thoughts/beliefs. All this goes to enhance believability (crucial for copy/persuasion).
  • Ask questions throughout your copy (questions the reader has) to keep their interest - If you start writing too much without asking and answering the question the reader is having at the moment they’re having it, then they lose interest. But if you can ask the question at the right time, then you keep their interest (because now you’re creating a two-way conversation instead of just a one-way conversation. In other words, you’re making this an active experience instead of a passive experience because the reader is now a participant in the unfoldment of the copy/story).
  • Communicate a lot in as little space as possible - Say what you need to say and nothing more. Read the snippet from the book ‘Women’ again. Notice how much is conveyed in just a single paragraph. Impressive, isn’t it? This is the true power behind Bukowski. He wrote short chapters, but conveyed a lot of rich detail because each word, sentence, paragraph meant something. If it didn’t mean something, it was axed. How much of your copy actually means something? How much of it is just extra seasoning? Get rid of the seasoning and keep the meat. Remember, you want to strip away the clothes and keep your writing naked. Otherwise you’ll bore people and make them stop reading. Think of writing like a movie. If a movie is presenting you with one meaningful scene after the next that builds on each other, then you keep watching. But if there’s a bunch of random nonsense, you lose interest. The same applies to writing.

How do you do this?

By using the bare minimum words required to present the information you want to convey. For example, let’s say you were wanting to tell the story of how you lost 10lbs by doing the Keto Diet and improved your dating life in the process. Here’s how one copywriter would go about it:

“The Keto Diet is absolutely amazing! In fact, if it wasn’t for the Keto Diet, then chances are I’d still be carrying an extra 10lbs around my waistline and getting stares of pity from the females I fancied. But that’s not the case anymore because even though I resisted the Keto Diet at first, I came around to it.

Let me explain…

In the beginning, I thought Keto was… [continues as a long winded, overly drawn out story]

Notice how this lacks punch, believability, and uses a bunch of unnecessary words that aren’t meaningful, but hypey?

If I was to put on my Charles Bukowski hat here, I’d fix this copy by writing the following:

“I hated Keto. It seemed annoying. Too demanding. Even a bit cultish. But I had to do something. I was tired of seeing my boys leave the club with beautiful women they had just met while I remained fat and alone. Fisting my drink in the corner.

So I did. My friend Rick recommended it. He said it was the most effective diet he ever tried. I didn’t believe him. Sounded like another BS fad diet to me.

But I trusted Rick. He was a good guy.

So I gave it a shot.

And the results? Well, I’m not one to brag, but here’s how I used to look and here’s how I look now: <pictures>

Doesn’t even look like the same guy, does it?

That’s what beautiful women tell me all the time now.

But how exactly did I make this happen?

After all, I wasn’t too fond of Keto in the beginning… it was difficult, draining, and even made me hate meat for a while.

But I found a secret that made Keto bearable.

Not easy. But doable.

What was the secret?

My secret was...”

Mind you I just wrote this off the top of my head with no editing, but notice how that is much more engaging, believable, and draws you in because it has a stronger pace to it? Such is the power of the Bukowski way of writing.

Alright can you now see how implementing the above tips makes your writing simpler and irresistible?

If you can simply combine the above with what you learn through direct response and you practice executing both on the daily, then your writing will not only become more persuasive, but irresistible to read. This is the winning combo.

That’s my little secret for writing irresistible copy. (Side note: I actually think the reason Gary Halbert was so successful is because he was the perfect blend of Charles Bukowski and direct response copywriter. Go read the Gary Halbert letters and you’ll see this clear as day. His writing is simple, honest, and fast paced. Just like Charles. Here’s one of my favourite first liners from Gary: “I didn’t have to masturbate to get my heart started this morning.” Very Bukowski-like, isn’t it? Here’s another: “I bet Bill Gates never had a good piece of ass in his life.” Same effect, isn’t it? Hopefully you’re starting to see the connection here and just how powerful your writing can become if you combine direct response copywriting principles with the Bukowski way of writing especially when it comes to telling stories in your copy.)

What Bukowski books do I recommend for analysing/copying stories into your own writing?

Post Office, Ham On The Rye, Women.

Those are my favourite Bukowski books and they’ll teach you all you need to know about writing irresistible stories. First, read the books from start to finish and truly experience the effect it has on you. Then handwrite a couple short stories into your own writing and analyse what makes the writing so effective. Then implement your learnings using your own takeaways, plus the ‘Bukowski Effect’ checklist above and your writing will come to life and people won’t be able to stop reading. This is all it takes. So apply yourself and make it happen.
