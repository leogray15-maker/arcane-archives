SALES TRAINING 101

Sales How to Negotiate Better Than 95% of People

If you didn’t know this, NOW is your chance to increase your sales skills.

All of life is sales.

Asking someone out on a date is selling them on yourself.

Trying to convince someone to hang out is selling yourself.

Starting a business is, of course, sales.

And you’ll never get better at sales if you can’t negotiate.

Negotiation is all about understanding what you want, what the other person wants and using that to get to your destination.

Everyone talks about common negotiation tips, like active listening, getting people to say “YES” and using specific numbers.

But here are ones you might not have heard that will make a massive difference:

1. Use these 2 tips to control any negotiation:

I’m sure you’ve heard this trope before:

“Whoever needs the other person less has more power in any situation.”

Here’s what that means broken down even further:

Whoever is in more of a hurry seems to be more out of control.

So how do you show control over the situation?

There are 2 ways:

  1. Ask open-ended questions in the beginning of the negotiation
  1. Use strategic pauses when you speak

Let’s break them down:

Point 1:

One of the main keys in negotiation is to listen more than you speak.

But there’s only one way to get people to keep on speaking.

That’s by asking open-ended questions.

If you keep leading them down “yes” or “no” questions, they get to keep their cards close to their chest and guard info they may not want to relinquish.

However, if you ask them open-ended questions, they must provide explanations surrounding the questions you’ve asked.

This makes it more likely they give info away that could be useful to you, and also allows you to look for clues that can help you get to where you want to go.

Example:

You were passed over unjustly to lead a work project in favour of your newer coworker, who isn’t as skilled as you are.

There is overwhelming evidence that you were the right person for the job, but were not selected.

Instead of asking your boss:

“Do you think (coworker’s name) is the right person for the job?” (This pits you against your coworker and a good boss would never put down an employee.)

You can ask them something like:

“Can you describe in detail what skill set you think would best fit this project?”

(Now they open up and explain what skills are needed to make sure the process goes as well as possible.)

Point 2:

The best way to control the situation is by slowing down.

And if you want to slow things down, you must not only speak in a slow, controlled tone.

You must use pauses when you speak and after you ask questions.

Pauses make people uncomfortable.

Most people don’t want to awkwardly sit in silence, so they rush to fill in the gaps.

When they do that, it leaves them susceptible again to parlaying info they would’ve kept secret if they were in a more controlled mental state.

Example:

Back to our example from above, when you ask your boss the question, STOP SPEAKING after you ask it.

Don’t start speaking after you ask them the question.

2. Get to “NO” instead of “YES.”

This is the biggest negotiation misconception there is.

There was even a book written about it:

“Getting to Yes” by Roger Fisher.

However, lots of people can lie when they say “yes.”

If you didn’t like doing school work as a teenager, how many times did you placate your parents or teachers?

“Did you study?”

“Yeah, for sure.” (You didn’t.)

On the flip side, most people are much less likely to lie with no.

Ask your friend who says they want a family but can’t stop having one-night stands at the bar on weekends:

“Do you really want to be single in your 40s when you could have a family?”

Most likely they will respond with an emphatic “NO.”

In fact, in 2002 a book was written on this as well (“Start with No”).

This is also talked about in Chris Voss’ book about negotiation: “Never Split the Difference.”

When you get people saying “no,” they’re far more likely to be truthful about their intentions.

You use this after you use open-ended questions to open them up.

The reason for this is because once you’ve got them speaking, you can now narrow down your focus and show them their blindspots.

Example:

Let’s continue with our example from the first point, shall we?

Once you’ve asked your boss to describe the skills needed to take on the project, you can probe further.

Ask them what their process was for finding someone to take the project and get them to explain more.

Now comes looking for “no.”

Ask them if they feel like, based on the description, if your coworker is the best fit for the project.

If they try to waffle around the point, you can mention that this isn’t a putdown of anyone, just a simple question of if they’re the best fit of that description.

Once they admit “no,” you can go one step further.

You can ask if they believe by not putting the most qualified person in charge, if that’s what’s best for the client.

When you get “no” again, you can go for a third.

You can ask your boss if giving the client less than the best service possible is what the company strives for.

Three “no” answers.

3. Use a Higher Ask as an “Anchor.”

Anchoring is a process where you ask for a much higher number in order to make the real asking price seem more reasonable in comparison.

You see online purchases do this all the time.

They give you much higher figures on shorter subscription offers so you choose the longer deal at a lower monthly fee (resulting in more money for them).

While some people look down on this strategy, saying it might be “unethical,” here’s the reality.

Most people you negotiate with are going to try to pay you less than they have to.

If you say a price and someone goes “yes” right away, you might’ve been able to get it for higher (which could leave a bad taste in your mouth.

Instead, if they aren’t willing to go as high and you lower the price to where they’re comfortable, (assuming you are cool with it) you are maxing out the value they’re willing to give you.

Always go for as much as you can.

It brings out your best work and increases their level of investment.

That’s it!
