A Landing Page Copywriting Framework.

A Landing Page Copywriting Framework That Gets Results.

I am not a professional copywriter, nor have I done it for freelance work — but I have seen great success with my sales pages.

I have taken copy courses, gotten tips from freelance copywriters, and have tested numerous ways of crafting sales pages. What you will find below is a streamlined way of doing so.

Start to think of your sales page as a way of structuring your marketing strategy. You will be trying to effectively communicated your offer and all of your marketing firepower from the course you just went through.

Persuasive Writing Structures

There are 3 writing structures I want you to keep in mind.

  • AIDA
    • Attention, Interest, Desire, Action
  • PAS
    • Problem, Agitate, Solution
  • PASTOR - read more here
    • Problem, Amplify, Story/Solution, Transformation/Testimony, Offer, Response

The common theme with these is using psychological triggers to keep the reader engaged, communicate the value of your offer, and make them convert.

While I do not treat any of these as law, you should keep their general structure in mind.

  • Is your headline holding attention?
  • Are you painting a picture of their big problem(s) relating to your solution?
  • Are you agitating / amplifying that problem?
  • Do you have a soft story / transformation that relates to their situation?
  • Do you introduce your offer / solution in an enticing way?
  • Do you have a strong call to action?
  • Are you holding the readers attention up until that call to action?

How To Write Your Copy

As with most things, I would highly HIGHLY recommend brain dumping beforehand. Do not try to write the final draft in one go.

Outline, brainstorm, go about your life, let your subconscious work on your copy for you, and take note of things that come to mind as you go about your days.

This is especially true for your headlines, lead, and offer introduction. Write down as many ideas as you can. Force them out of your head. Then come back to them a few days later with more subconscious ammunition.

The Hero Section (Above The Fold)

There are 4 aspects of a great hero section. For this entire "Hero Section" you will be including as much of your marketing firepower as possible.

A reminder of your marketing firepower:

  • Your USP
  • Your quantifiable end result (or timeframe if you have trouble with QER)
  • Your promise / solution
  • Your target audience (calling them out catches attention)
  • A hint at your unique system/mechanism
  • The big problem
  • Risk reversal / guarantee

It is up to you to treat these as puzzle pieces, brainstorm, and piece together the perfect hero section using these 4 things:

1) The Eyebrow (optional)

The eyebrow is the optional text above your headline. Usually small and hardly noticeable. This is where I like to call out my target audience.

2) The Headline

This is the most important aspect of all of your copy. I typically like to include my promise/solution, aspects of my USP, my QER, and a brief mention of one of their problems.

3) The Subheadline

I use the subheadline to touch on anything else I missed. Mostly the risk reversal and unique mechanism.

4) The CTA Button

It only makes sense to have a call to action button in this area. It looks good and gives people the option to buy upfront if they are ready.

The Lead

The lead section is where you introduce the problem and agitate it. This can be very simple or complex. Here are 2 things you can include:

1) Social Proof

I have seen great copywriters recommend including social proof up front. I think it is a smart move IF you have a lot of social proof. It builds trust from the beginning and can be used to handle initial objections.

Include 2-3 strong testimonials.

2) The Lead

My favorite way of doing this is simple. Bullet points.

When in doubt, bullet points are your best friend. I see it as a way of hitting multiple pain-points and painting a picture of where they are currently at.

It is like a list-style tweet — if people relate with one point, they will like, retweet, and possibly follow (they will keep reading your copy).

The key to these is to get as specific as possible. Dig into your market research from the Advanced Marketing Roadmap. Dig into your past experiences. Brain dump and iterate on what you write down.

Of course, if you need to, have a few sentences before and after the lead to transition nicely from the hero section and into the body.

The Body

The body section is where you include your story, transformation, and solution. This can be in the form of a personal story, a specific client success story, or just a brief explanation of the solution.

I wouldn't overcomplicate this section too much. It does not have to be a certain character or word count.

However, I would try to handle as many objections as possible while telling this story. Refer back to your market research, understand what is going through your customer's mind as they are reading, and handle their objections in your copy.

At the start of this body section you should include a headline. I treat headlines as the big idea behind the entire section. In short, summarize the most compelling points of the body section as the headline.

If your body talks about a client that went from overweight to having a six-pack, your headline could be: How John Lost His Love Handles While Eating Pizza Everday

The Offer Introduction

From what I've heard, your offer introduction should take up no more than 20% of your landing page. (That is, 20% of the ~600 words it will take to get to the end of the main call to action).

As with all major sections in your copy, include a headline at the start. Something as simple as "Introducing [Product Name]" works fine.

Keep this brief, use benefits rich language, use bullet points, and use the bullet points in your lead to generate ideas on how to best word these. Your offer introduction should act as a solution to the problems you listed in your lead.

Another thing — you should run through your product / offer outline and write down what the reader actually wants out of each section. Benefits over features.

If you have an ebook and one chapter is on "how to count calories and be in a caloric deficit"

You should write it as: The stupid simple way of guaranteeing weight loss (even if you were to eat a big mac everyday)

You can think of these bullets as mini-headlines. With that, I would study how people write headlines. Watch some YouTube videos and read blog articles on it.

After you introduce different aspects of your offer, you can include another section of bullet points where you introduce the bonuses that come along with it.

Social Proof

Again, have another headline. "Praise From Our 500+ Customers" or something along those lines work.

Then, of course, list off all of the social proof you have. If you don't have any, don't worry — but make it your full time job to get social proof.

Call To Action

The call to action is not as simple as a button. There are multiple things you can include here.

1) Display Of Value

I like comparing the value of what I am offering to what is currently on the market. Are you giving away information that usually costs more?

Can you break down the what they get relating to the price? (If you are offering a solution to getting a six pack, paint a picture of what that is worth to someone... is a $47 investment worth having your wife look at you with googly eyes?)

2) Giving Them 2 Options

Remind yourself of the main points in your lead (the pain points) and the main points of your offer (the benefits / solution).

Paint a brief picture of where they are now, and give them the option to stay where they are or transform their life.

How you word this is up to you. You don't want to be like, "You can either keep your sh*tty life or buy my product and change it."

That's just going to lose conversions lol. Have some self-awareness and toy with it a bit.

That's It! (Kinda)

If you are writing copy for a Gumroad landing page, you can stop here.

Your main call to action should come quick. Display your value with the info above in about 500-700 words and end it there (Gumroad doesn't allow more than one call to action).

If you are creating a page on Carrd, WordPress, or another software that allows you to have CTA buttons anywhere — you can end the copy here or consider it extra.

Now we can dive into the extras that come after the main call to action.

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything that comes after is about objection handling and possibly hitting different marketing angles for those that are unsure about your offer.

FAQ's are pretty straightforward, but the questions should be crafted in a persuasive way.
