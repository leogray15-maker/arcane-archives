We have our product idea, customer avatar, offer information, and the start of a product outline.

Now we need to learn how to take all of that, put it on a simple landing page, and start selling before the product is even ready.

What??

Yes.

I would not recommend pouring a ton of time into creating a great product when you don't even know if people are going to buy it.

We'll talk about launch and promotion strategies over the next few days, but here's the general process:

  • Write the copywriting for the landing page (copywriting, for those unaware, is persuasive writing - like written sales)
  • Have a slight discount for early bird buyers
  • Set a launch date as a deadline
  • When people buy it, have a module or information stating that the product will launch on a specific date and to keep an eye on their email
  • Start promoting to your audience
  • If it sells, even a small amount, you can decide to commit further and build it out before the launch date

People are less likely to buy weeks out from the launch date, but some people will to secure the discount. Even if a few people buy, that's a good enough sign to build it since it only gets easier to sell from there.

Now, copywriting and building the landing page is easy once you have the pieces of the offer down.

The entire point of copywriting is to illustrate the offer in a clear and persuasive way.

We have the persuasive part down (problem, desired outcome, timeframe, personal system, etc) now we need the structure.

The best way to build and host these products, again, is on a platform like Stan.

On Stan, the landing pages are just plain text. You don't need to worry about learning web design or any of that, but we do need to learn what to write on that page. Plus, all of your links are products live on a LinkTree style page which is perfect for promoting in your bio, posts, and newsletters.

Headline

Your headline and sub headline should be a condensation of all the value inside of your offer.

Your job is to combine the most potent parts of the big problem, desired outcome, personal system, timeframe, and potentially the target persona in this section.

For a productivity product, the headline could be:

Study For 12 Hours A Day Without Adderall

Subheadline

In the subheadline, you add the other parts.

Since I only included the desired outcome and timeframe in the headline, because it was the most potent, I could make my subheadline:

Use “The Attention Marathon” system to avoid a low-paying 9-5 and conquer cheap dopamine.

There I pull in the personal system, big problem, and even toss in a high performing topic like cheap dopamine.

Your headline and subheadline should get your offer across as clear as possible.

Problem + Amplify

In the next section most people call the lead, your job is to illustrate the big problem and negative outcome.

When you start with the problem, you increase the reader’s awareness around the problem. You give them the belief that their life can change (because it can) by using a system that helped you.

Personally, I like to include a few short sentences that state the problem, then follow that with a bullet point list of pain points that stem from that problem.

You can visit any of my landing pages to see this in action.

If you don’t know what to write here, use your personal story.

Where were you before?

Why did you want to change?

What did you try that didn’t work?

Personal System

Moving into the next section of the landing page, your job is to show how your personal system is different.

What was the turning point in your story?

How did you discover and create the personal system?

Why is it better than most other solutions on the market?

If you can add a graphic here to help explain the system, even better.

Results or Testimonials

After the personal system, you can throw in images of results people have gotten with your product.

If you don’t have testimonials, you have two options:

  • Walk people through your product for free in exchange for a testimonial
  • Show your own results

You can use images of yourself (like for productivity or health), or you can use screenshots if your topic is something digital like web design.

Features + Benefits

This is the “offer introduction” section.

Here, you break down every thing they receive inside the program.

The simplest way to do this is just a bullet point list with the features + benefits.

If it helps, think of this list as the opposite of the list of pain points you included in the lead.

You’ve probably seen this before in most products you’ve purchased. It’s just a list of what’s inside + why they should care.

Call To Action

After the features and benefits section you need to close the deal.

I like to do a comparison.

Something like “You before this product” vs “You after this product” that reiterate the pain points and benefits.

This can be a graphic or a simple bullet point list.

If you are using something like Stan, let this go straight into the payment button. Skip the FAQ section in this case.

FAQ (Optional)

You probably shouldn't have an FAQ on a Stan landing page.

The benefit of using Stan is that it is built for conversions. It's just pure persuasive text that leads to a payment form.

You don't want to add any unnecessary information before the payment form.

When you design your own landing page, you can add sections after the call to action like an FAQ.

But when you're limited to a short amount of text, you usually want to focus on:

  • Getting to the Call to Action in 750 words or less
  • Hit the problem > outcome > system quick and efficiently

For now, I wouldn't worry about the FAQ.

Creating A Landing Page With AI

I like to think of AI as a prototyping tool.

It's not great at producing perfectly polished marketing material, but it is an incredible first step to get you 80-90% of the way there. Then, you use your skill and taste to refine the other 10%.

The thing about creating a landing page is that there are so many different frameworks and strategies to create one. You also have to account for what type of product you are creating. A software landing page has a much different vibe than an ecommerce or digital product page.

For that reason, it would be better to find someone else's landing page that (1) performs well and (2) sells the same type of product as you.

From there, you can do what we've done many times before by writing a prompt like:

Break down the structure of this landing page into a copywriting framework. Provide details such as psychological biases used, transitions, and methods used to hold attention. Create a step-by-step guide for me to replicate this landing page copy with my own product.

[paste the landing page copywriting here]

The main thing you're trying to get out of this step is the structure of the landing page.

Since you are creating a digital product, you can use my own personal structure to save you time researching. I would create a new note called "How To Write Landing Page Copywriting," and paste the below text into it:

The Landing Page Structure

  • Main Headline (3 variations): Incorporate the big idea, problem, timeframe, or unique mechanism in an attention-grabbing way.
  • Sub-headline (3 variations): Build on the headline by including compelling aspects not covered in the main headline.
  • Lead: Amplify the problem and pain points leading to negative outcomes. Use bullet points with line breaks (empty lines) between each.
  • Transformation Section with Headline: Create a headline using direct response principles that hints at transformation. Include a compelling illustration of a personal, client, or reader transformation, using the unique mechanism as the achievement method.
  • Testimonial Section Placeholder: Indicate where testimonials can be added.
  • Offer Introduction Section with Headline: Create a headline stating the desired outcome. Showcase product features with benefits-rich language, focusing on reader benefits over features.
  • Call to Action: Remind readers of the transformation—contrast where they are now with where they could be. Include price and risk reversal.

Before we continue making a prompt that generates a landing page, there is one more trick we can employ.

Copywriting is an art, and we can pull advice from legendary copywriters such as Eugene Schwartz, Gary Halbert, or David Ogilvy to help with writing the actual page.

Try out this prompt:

Research the book Breakthrough Advertising and give me a detailed step-by-step guide on how to write persuasive copywriting.

That prompt alone will teach you more than I could ever teach you on writing good copy.

Now, save the output of that prompt in the same "How To Write Landing Page Copywriting" under the text you already have in there.
