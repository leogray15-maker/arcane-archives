DAY 14 & 15 - WHY NOBODY OPENS YOUR NEWSLETTERS

Over the past 3 days, we've learned about outlining the newsletter, pulling inspiration from others, and how to write a great intro without flavor-of-the-day hook tactics you'll find online.

Technically, you should have everything you need to write a great newsletter.

If you ever feel stuck, you can always ask AI for 5 options on ways to continue writing, or you can refer back to day 12 to learn how to emulate someone else's newsletter to get unstuck.

However, I want to make this a bit more concrete.

I want to give you 3 newsletter frameworks, from beginner to advanced, so you have multiple ways to practice writing.

The introduction for each newsletter will largely be the same across them all. That's what we learned yesterday.

Structuring the sections or key points of the newsletter is a different story.

Framework 1) Pain & Process

This is the beginner level framework.

It's pretty simple.

First, you illustrate the pain point in the introduction. We know how to do this.

Second, you give an actionable process to solve the problem. This usually comes in the form of step-by-step advice.

Here's an example:

  • Pain point: You don't know what you want in life and it leads to inaction and anxiety
  • Step 1: Realize what you don't want by creating an anti-vision
  • Step 2: Set a series of goals that will move you away from that
  • Step 3: Turn those goals into projects and learn how to learn
  • Step 4: Timeblock 60 minutes for learning and 60 minutes for building

Pretty simple.

Then, you fill in the steps with big ideas, examples, personal stories, quotes, more pain points, and other things until you amply explain each step.

You can either write out the steps as one big section after the introduction, or separate them by headings to make it a bit more readable.

I'd recommend having a heading for each step for the sake of readability.

If you go to Medium.com, you'll notice that most articles look like this.

Note: The "steps" in the framework can be anything. It's a list of things that help resolve the pain point. It can be "5 steps" or 5 reasons, tips, insights, lessons or anything else that can be turned into a list.

Framework 2) Pain, Concept, Process

This is the intermediate level framework.

In a nutshell, we add one extra section to add greater depth and novelty to the newsletter.

This makes the newsletter longer, which may not be your cup of tea, but this is how most of my newsletters are written.

In order to do this well, you need to have a reading or research habit. If you don't have a deep concept to write about – which is usually found in books or other curated content – then this one is difficult to write.

Building on our last example:

  • Pain point: You don't know what you want in life and it leads to inaction and anxiety
  • Concept: Emotional transmutation and how to turn negative energy into positive action
  • Step 1: Realize what you don't want by creating an anti-vision
  • Step 2: Set a series of goals that will move you away from that
  • Step 3: Turn those goals into projects and learn how to learn
  • Step 4: Timeblock 60 minutes for learning and 60 minutes for building

Your introduction would include the pain point.

Then, you'd write a section headline and explain the concept of emotional transmutation.

Then, you'd write another section headline and explain the step-by-step process, separated by smaller subheadings.

The reason I love this framework is because I tend to have an idea or concept I want to write about first.

Most of the time, I don't start with the topic or pain point, I start with a big idea or concept that I think is worth sharing, then I build the pain point and process around the concept.

For this example, I could be reading some philosophy book or watching a video, come across the concept of emotional transmutation, want to write about it, and start fleshing out a newsletter outline like the one above to make it more practical.

This works well because emotional transmutation adds a layer of depth to the process section.

In step 1, I talk about creating an anti-vision to build up negative energy. That's a great idea on its own, but when you preface it with some kind of philosophical or scientific concept, people thank you for how impactful your newsletter was.

Framework 3) Perspective, Advantage, Gamify

This is the advanced level framework.

It's roughly the same as the others, except you exchange the "concept" with a "secret advantage."

For this framework:

  • Perspective = how most people view the problem or pain point. The common way of looking at it.
  • Advantage = how you look at the problem or pain point and what a better way of doing it is.
  • Gamify = a step by step process that follows flow psychology, you turn it into a game

By all measures, this framework is the most powerful because you change their mind and life in one piece of writing because you have a deep understanding of behavior psychology.

I don't want to overwhelm you with details just yet, but I would recommend reading the book Flow by Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi and attempting to apply it to your writing. Rather than using it to create flow in your own life, use it to create flow in others'.

In short, the "gamify" section is the same as the "process" section, just broken down into tiny challenging steps for the specific person who is reading, making the action steps more tangible and doable. You remove as much friction as you can between the problem and the action.

Using our last example:

  • Perspective: How most people view getting what they want in life
  • Advantage: How you view getting what you want in life
  • Step 1: Pull out a notebook
  • Step 2: Write down the most painful experience in your life
  • Step 3: Write down everything you hate about your current life
  • Step 4: The single habit that will change everything

This is very similar to the last framework, but it is slightly more effective because it gives them no option but to change and act.

Your action step today is to continue writing your newsletter.

If you are struggling, consider rewriting your outline quickly to match the first Pain & Process framework.

In your next newsletters, attempt to challenge yourself by trying different frameworks listed here.

Day 15 – Why Nobody Opens Your Newsletters (or Videos)

By this point, you have a written newsletter.

If you haven't, pretend that you have, because that's when this lesson will apply to you.

So far, you have the topic of the newsletter, but that may not be the best title for it.

It may not be the most attention-grabbing title there is, and if people don't click on your newsletter (or click on your YouTube video if you turn this into a script), then a lot of your hard work goes to waste.

This is where packaging comes into play.

In short, nobody wants to open an ugly cardboard box filled with junk.

However, people are willing to pay good money for an Apple product has a great unboxing experience.

You already have the inside of the package organized to be perceived as valuable once they open it, but getting them to open it is 80% of the battle.

Now, as you may have noticed so far, I don't really care for flavor-of-the-day tricks and hacks. I care about what works, experimentation as a personality trait, and constant learning. If anyone can give you the latest and greatest "viral tactic," more people will use it, and eventually it won't work anymore. What do you do after that? What do you do if you always rely on the latest and greatest tactic?

Emulating What Works As Training Wheels

As you'll notice, this is the exact same process we used when we started writing posts. It's also what we used to break down and understand other people's newsletter structure.

Here's what you do:

  • Immerse yourself in newsletter, YouTube, and article titles that perform well
  • Create a new note or document where you can save these
  • Break them down and study them to learn why they work
  • Attempt to replicate them with your own titles
  • Experiment with your own original titles when they come to mind

You can study books and courses on how to write good headlines and titles, but over time, the simple act of experimentation will teach you everything you need to know, faster than any other route.

I would personally recommend using YouTube, Medium, or Substack to research good titles to save and emulate.

Medium and Substack work well, but you usually have to scroll on that platform every day just to discover titles that are worth saving.

YouTube, on the other hand, is a search engine.

It also has an algorithm that consistently exposes you to high-performing videos around the topics you watch.

You may not realize it yet, but this lesson inside the challenge is my entire YouTube growth strategy in a nutshell. The newsletter is my script. I research YouTube titles that perform well. I adapt my newsletter title to those. When I post my YouTube video, it is much more likely to do well.

Why?

  • If a video is going viral, YouTube recommends similar videos to the people who watched it
  • Since a lot of people watched it, you are that much more likely to show up in their recommended sections (on the sidebar while watching the video and in their homepage)
  • Since many YouTubers do this, that only means you will show up in more people's recommended section
  • And of course, the title is good enough to click. You don't have to think much about that

In terms of how I edit my YouTube videos, I watch YouTube videos and screenshot the things that I like about them (the background music, pacing, hook, b-roll, cuts, images, colors, etc). I experiment with them in my own videos, and over time, I have such a mixture of great screens that I'm confident my videos are better than if I weren't do that. Creativity also springs up within this process.

When it comes to your newsletter, you can be fairly confident that by emulating a title that is already performing well, it will perform well for you – just like with social media posts.

So how do you do this?

This may sound familiar:

  • Write down 3-5 creators who have topics within your content map
  • If you can't find any, look up topics within your content map and look for high-performing videos
  • Go to the creator's account, click on their videos tab, and sort by most popular
  • Write down at least 5 titles from each creator that you could see yourself using out of their popular videos

Now you have a list of 15 to 25 high-performing titles that you can mix and match from.

What you'll find is that most titles are like templates. They are composed of an idea and a structure. So, if you can break down that structure, you can plug the best parts of your newsletter into it.

As an example, one of my best-performing videos is "The One-Person Business (How To Productize Yourself)"

If we break that down, we realize that "The One-Person Business" is a novel reframe of something like freelancing or being a solopreneur. It's an identity-based concept. We also realize that "How To Productize Yourself" is an interesting frame of a process like turning yourself into a business.

If we take the example from yesterday where I outlined emotional transmutation and creating an "anti-vision" I could come up with a title like:

The Complete Life Reset (How To Turn Negative Emotions Into Success)

I could have just put "The Anti-Vision" in the title, but that doesn't feel right. That's what most people would put as the title unless they thought it through. "The One-Person Business" does well because it's an aspiration. It's something that people can do that is associated with things like more money.

"The Complete Life Reset" took some thinking and creativity to come up with, but you can tell it gets the point across better than the other option. It's something that people can do in order to get an end result of more clarity.

Most of the time, if your content doesn't do well when you are emulating someone else's title, it's because you didn't really understand why that title did well in the first place.

If you want to enhance this process with AI, we can create a prompt like this one I made for my own newsletter and YouTube titles (from my own best content).

First, send this prompt in a new chat:

Do a comprehensive analysis of these YouTube titles. The overall idea, how the titles are structured, and choice of words. Analyze why people click on the title, why it works so well, what psychological tactics are being used, and how I can replicate this style step-by-step with my own ideas.

[paste your list of 15-25 titles here]

Note: I'm saying "YouTube titles" here, but know that these are synonymous with newsletter titles.

Once you do that, save the output somewhere safe, like a new note or document.

Then, start a new chat, copy this prompt, paste it, and hit send. This is a prompt that helps you create better prompts.

I would recommend using Claude Sonnet for this and turning on "Extended Thinking" for this step.

It will respond with some clarifying questions to figure out what kind of prompt you want to create.

Simply tell it this:

I want to create a prompt that generates 10 high-performing YouTube titles based on the piece of long form content I give you (like a newsletter, script, or article). You will take the long-form content, determine it's most compelling ideas, and turn them into click-worthy titles.

Use this analysis to write the prompt:

[paste the output from the last prompt here]

That will spit out a prompt you can use to generate ideas for potential titles. Sometimes this works sometimes it doesn't. I wouldn't rely on it too heavily, but it works for inspiration sometimes.

If you look at my own prompt I linked above, you'll see that I added an "Examples" section under the "Instructions" section. If you'd like, you can paste your list of 15-25 titles into their as well for more accurate results.

That's it!

That's how you start practicing your packaging.
