DAY 3 - BUILD YOUR IDEA MUSEUM

Yesterday, you clarified the broad areas you'll write about. Today, you're going to fill those areas with real, usable ideas.

The goal is to create a running collection of high-quality ideas you can consistently turn into posts.

This is what professional creators do: they curate inspiration before they need it.

Why This Matters

The internet is noisy. AI is making it noisier.

If you want to stand out, your content needs to become a trusted source of signal — no fluff, no filler, just the most useful ideas in your own words.

Your account should become the go-to place for:

  • Clear thinking
  • Original insight
  • Curated value

This happens by collecting the best ideas and slowly turning them into content over time.

The Two Filters

You’ll curate ideas that meet at the intersection of:

  1. Performance – The idea has the potential to resonate or perform well (likes, views, engagement).
  1. Excitement – You actually care about the idea and want to write about it.

Performance = What others care about.

Excitement = What you care about.

Great content lives in the overlap.

Step 1: Set Up Your Idea Museum

This is where you'll store any idea worth saving.

Use whatever tool is most convenient:

  • Apple Notes
  • Notion
  • Google Docs
  • Obsidian
  • Kortex
  • A running text file
  • Even a physical notebook

The structure does not matter. What matters is that you build the habit:

When you see a good idea, save it immediately.

This habit compounds over time and prevents idea drought.

Step 2: Curate Based on Idea Density

Don’t save just anything. Save high-density ideas — ideas that are clear, useful, thought-provoking, or well-expressed.

Sources to consider:

  1. Old or niche books – These tend to have original, time-tested ideas.
  1. Curated blogs or summary accounts – Examples include Little Almanack, Navalism, The Daily Maxwell Reader.
  1. High-quality social accounts – Identify 3–5 people whose posts make you stop and think. Study them.

Avoid accounts or content that feel like filler. Focus on quality over quantity.

With time, your judgment will improve. You'll start noticing which ideas are worth capturing and which are noise.

Step 3: Use One Idea to Write a Post

Choose one idea from your museum. Write a post using the same process from Day 1:

  1. Pick the idea
  1. Ask 3 questions about it
  1. Free-write your response
  1. Rewrite as the reader
  1. Anticipate objections
  1. Format for impact

Then, post it.

This doesn’t need to be perfect. The habit is more important than the result right now.

Today’s Checklist

  • Identify 2 or more high-signal sources of content
  • Save 5 or more ideas into your idea museum
  • Choose 1 of those ideas, write a short-form post (under 500 characters), and publish it
