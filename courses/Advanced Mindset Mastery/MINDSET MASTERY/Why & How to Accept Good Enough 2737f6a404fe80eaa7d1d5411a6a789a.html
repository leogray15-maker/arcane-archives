A "good enough" finished product always beats a "perfect" idea—but how do you figure out it is in fact finished?

It was 3AM. I was exhausted.

But there I was, still sitting at my desk. Staring at the computer, scrambling through notes. I kept researching and adding more references to my final paper in college.

My work was already sound. I had interviews with experts. Solid bibliography. Well-reasoned arguments. But it wasn't perfect .

All for a college paper that was read by a whooping total of 3 people . Well, 4 if you count my mom...

At what point is "good enough" enough? Up to what point you are still working on your original idea?

Perfect Will Kill Your Execution

This obsession with polishing something to perfection is not restricted to college students. It is a problem that plagues artists, engineers, project managers. Pretty much anyone who's trying to build, create, or provide something.

I often heard music producers say you're never done mixing a song: you abandon it. Otherwise, you just keep looking for more to add to it.

In project management, this behavior is called gold plating. You've met all the requirements, but keep on further enhancing the product. The results can be tragic. You end up with a bloated deliverable, hard to use, and user-hostile. Instead of adding to it, you distorted the original idea.

As a translator, it's easy to fall into this trap. You start looking for the perfect word or expression. Soon you fall into a rabbit hole of meanings, semantics, idioms, and cultures.

However, my work as a simultaneous interpreter forced me to learn to live with "good enough". In the heat of the moment, you do not have time to consider, and ponder, and rethink, and research, and rework—you have to be fast.

The lecture is going on. The speaker is still going at it. They have not stopped, and neither can you. So good enough is the way to go .

I know what you will ask me now...

But What Is Good Enough?

Well, here is my answer for it. It is whenever the product, service, or word achieves its minimum intended purpose, without taking more time than needed.

Ok, that's still not a helpful answer, is it? Let's see if we can make it clearer. When I'm in the booth, doing simultaneous interpreting, a word is good enough when:

  1. It approximates the meaning and tone enough to be clear to the audience.
  1. I can get to it without stumbling, stuttering, or worse, freezing.

Interpreting happens in real-time, so you're under a constant sense of urgency . The deadline is always one or two seconds away.

This forces you to accept that whatever solution you have at the moment will be the best you have to offer. It is the best level of "good" you can aim for: minimal improvement from status quo .

How Do I Define That For Me?

Consider who is your audience and how far you can take them. As an interpreter, the people who are listening to me might have 0% understanding of the foreign language. I try to deliver 100%. If I deliver 80%, it's still amazing. But anything that takes them up from 0 and is not negative (such as me stuttering) is an improvement.

The same goes for any area. You can't teach a 402 class to 101 students.

Maybe you feel you're not delivering enough in an article for beginners. But it is possible that all you need to do is ease them into the topic. Give them the first steps. What is already obvious to you is not clear to them.

Set A Deadline To Avoid Gold-Plating

Another important concept from project management is Parkinson's Law: "work expands to fill the time available" .

Based on this law, if you have infinite time to do your work (i.e., no deadline), only two things can happen:

  1. You'll never start ("I'll do it tomorrow"); or
  1. You'll never finish ("I can make it better!").

So if you are serious about delivering on your project, define a clear, hard deadline . Particularly if you are doing this on your own, and not for a client who gave you a due date. You have to give yourself limited time to work on it. And hold yourself accountable. Ask a friend for help, if you need to.

Otherwise, you'll be forever spinning your wheels without leaving the same spot. Or you'll be driving aimlessly, looking for an elusive finish line that only exists in your head.

Push It Out

In Portuguese, we say "feito é melhor que perfeito" (done is better than perfect). I know it is said in English too, but it rhymes in Portuguese, and everyone knows rhymes are always true (right?).

You can always reiterate, repurpose, and adapt a finished product. You cannot use an unfinished, abstract idea.

So I encourage you to take a step back. Think from the perspective of your audience. Will your creation take them one step further on their journey? If so, it's time to push post, send, share, and let them enjoy it. Their response will be better feedback than the inner critic in your head.
