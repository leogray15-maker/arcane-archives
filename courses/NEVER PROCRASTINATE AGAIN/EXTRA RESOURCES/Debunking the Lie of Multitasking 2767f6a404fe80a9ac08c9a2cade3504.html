Debunking the Lie of Multitasking.

Massive amounts of time and money are spent to figure out how people can increase their productivity (which saves time and money).

Increasing your productivity is a worthy pursuit. But this search for increased productivity has led to us normalizing one of the biggest killers of productivity.

Multitasking . The act of doing more than one thing at a time.

I was recently helping a client prepare for a job interview. When I read over the job posting, one of the key requirements was the ability to multitask. This is a huge red flag .

Why is multitasking a lie? Because the human brain doesn't actually multitask.

Note: Yes, the brain can do two things at the same time in certain cases. You can walk and chew gum at the same time. You can sing and play guitar at the same time. But those cases specifically involve doing a task that is habitual and unconscious.

Imagine an old computer. The brain of that old computer is a single-core single-thread processor. The computer seems to be running many programs at a single time. But what's actually happening is this:

  • the processor works on Task A for a certain period of time • it context switches to Task B
  • it works on Task B for a certain period of time
  • it continues this context switching and working, going from Task C, Task D, and so on
  • it then context switches back to Task A, starting the cycle over again

We don't notice the context switches happening because the "certain period of time" is so small. The computer appears to be working on many tasks at the same time.

(note: the computers of today have multi-core multi-threaded processors, so they actually are able to work on many tasks at the same time)

In this context, your brain is a lot like that computer with a single-core single-thread processor. Let's say you're writing a report while you're in a Zoom meeting. You're multitasking, right? No . When you're "multitasking", your brain is not focusing 100% on the report and the meeting at the same time. Your brain is actually:

  • focusing on the report for a small amount of time
  • context switching to the meeting
  • focusing on the meeting for a small amount of time
  • context switching back to the report
  • and on and on until you finish the report or the meeting ends

Same as the computer, the time the context switches take are so small, it feels like you're doing two things at once. You think you're doing two things at one time, but you're actually only ever doing one thing at a time.

Every single context switch has an energy cost. An hour of multitasking involves a huge number of these context switches - meaning a huge energy cost. Ever had a busy day full of multitasking? You were exhausted at the end of it. The cost of context switching is a major reason why .

That's not all!

Your brain doesn't entirely switch from Task A to Task B. Your focus - your attention - is sticky, and part of it sticks to Task A for a time. The same thing happens when it switches from Task B back to Task A. Part of your attention sticks to Task A for a time. This is called attention residue .

So, let's review:

When people multitask, they think they're doing many things at one time.

In reality, when people multitask, they're never actually getting focused on any one thing.

The result? Your work takes more time and more energy and it has less quality.

Don't half-ass two things at a time. Whole-ass one thing at a time .
