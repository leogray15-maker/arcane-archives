35. Lowering the Importance

The "Lowering the Importance" principle is all about shifting your relationship to your feelings and the way you perceive the significance of things in your life. It's a powerful concept because, as Zeland points out, most of the problems we face don’t come from the situations themselves but from how much importance we attach to them. This importance inflates the weight of things, making us feel anxious, stressed, or overwhelmed. But when we lower this importance, the problems often dissipate or lose their power over us.

First off, it's not about suppressing your emotions or denying what you're feeling. It's about changing your attitude toward the things that are triggering those feelings. For example, if someone gets upset about a small misunderstanding, it’s not the misunderstanding itself that’s the issue; it’s the importance they’ve given it. When we inflate the importance of something, it creates emotional turbulence. We start reacting with feelings like anger, frustration, or even fear, because we’ve made the situation seem more significant or threatening than it really is.

Zeland stresses that problems don’t exist inherently—they are a result of us elevating something to an undue level of importance. When you lower the importance of a problem, it ceases to feel like a burden. It’s not that you ignore it or pretend it doesn’t exist, but you approach it from a neutral, almost playful stance. It becomes less about "me against the world" and more like a challenge or a game that you’re observing and navigating, not one you’re overwhelmed by.

Let’s say something unexpected happens: you miss a train, or you receive a harsh critique at work. If you raise the importance of these events—thinking they will ruin your day or determine your worth—they become problems. But if you take a step back, lower the significance you attach to them, you see them for what they really are: small bumps in the road, manageable and temporary. You allow yourself to remain neutral rather than emotionally charged.

Zeland uses the example of people who go through major life events, like getting married or having a child. While these are significant moments, the key is not inflating their importance to a point where it causes stress or anxiety. A person who doesn’t get overly invested in these moments doesn’t “torture” themselves or others by making a huge emotional deal out of them. They can enjoy the event, but without the pressure of needing it to be perfect or defining their entire life.

Lowering the importance also removes the energetic hooks that pendulums (society, social expectations, group dynamics) can latch onto. Pendulums thrive on the emotional charge that comes from inflated importance—when we care too much, they pull us into their orbit, causing us to react in ways that aren’t true to ourselves. By staying neutral, we don’t feed the pendulum, and it loses its grip over us.

Ultimately, it’s about learning to observe rather than react . When you stop inflating the importance of events, you approach life with more clarity and peace. You are no longer emotionally controlled by the ups and downs of daily life. You become the calm center in a world that constantly tries to stir up emotional storms. By lowering the importance, you allow yourself to move through life with ease and to respond with wisdom, not overreaction.

This doesn’t mean becoming emotionless or detached—it’s about balance. It’s about understanding that not everything in life needs to be a crisis or a drama. By embracing this neutral, relaxed approach, you find yourself in a state of equilibrium, and from this place, you can navigate the world with much more control and purpose.
