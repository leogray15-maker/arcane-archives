Copywriting is easily the aspect of marketing that stresses the most people out.

People overcomplicate it because:

1) They don’t know what it is

2) They follow copywriters that make it sound mystical and reserved for people that have handwritten thousands of sales letters

In short, copywriting is how you effectively communicate your marketing strategy on a landing page.

We’ve already written out our marketing strategy, you are 80% done.

Now, all you need to do is put it into sentences, brainstorm aspects of your headline, and understand how to make it flow.

In terms of being good at copywriting, I wouldn’t put too much pressure on yourself.

The depth of your content, quality of the product, quantity of promotions, and amount of social proof will do most of the heavy lifting.

In-depth copywriting is there to push people that aren’t already going to buy over the edge by clearing their doubts.

Keep in mind that these work for both product and service offers. High or low ticket.

Structuring Your Copy

If you've never written copy before, I would recommend starting with the PASTOR framework.

Read more about it here before continuing.

This will help you understand the flow of your copy.

There are TONS of other frameworks out there... if you do some research and find which one you like better, feel free to use it.

Also, it is incredibly important that you read the copy of your competitors and copywriting in general. Pay attention to their writing style, how they structure the benefits, and how they seamlessly transition from one section to the next.

Understanding the PASTOR framework is good, but you need to understand how to display this on a landing page. Here is the structure we will be following:

  1. Eyebrow (audience callout or big idea)
  1. Headline (include the clear goal)
  1. Subheadline (fill in with other aspects of your marketing)
  1. Social proof 1 (optional and highest authority testimonials)
  1. Lead (qualifying pain points)
  1. Body (transformation story & unique mechanism)
  1. Offer introduction (features + benefits rich language)
  1. Social proof 2 (customer testimonials)
  1. Risk Reversal and/or guarantee
  1. Call to action (reminder of where they are and where they want to be)
  1. FAQ (self-explanatory)
  1. Objection handling + stories + more CTAs (optional)

We want numbers 1-11 to be rapid fire. Get the readers to the call to action and keep it within 600-800 words.

One mistake I made when writing "long-form" copy is that I delayed the offer introduction and call to action for a LONG time.

You can still have long-form copy after 1-11, but we don't want to lose readers because of this.

As a beginner, I would stay away from long-form copy. Stick to numbers 1-11 and iterate on them a few times before adding more to your page.

How Your Niche & Marketing Strategy Come Into Play

ALL of these sections can be as short or as long as you’d like. It took me a week to write the copy for my first course, now I can write intricate long form copy. It really is just a matter of practice, but listen to me when I say that one isn’t better than the other . Short form is preferred in a lot of cases, especially if your other content is on point and valuable.

If you don't know what to write, act as if you are presenting an argument to yourself as to why you should care (since you are the customer avatar).

Write through the lens of where you were in the past and how you wanted to be where you are now. What would convince you?

Let’s walk through how the pieces fit in like building blocks:

1) The Eyebrow

The eyebrow is at the very top of the page. This is where I like to include a big idea or a specific and short description of who I can help the most from my marketing strategy. This should capture attention and open a curiosity loop.

2) The Headline

The entire “above the fold” section (what you can see when the page first loads) should include MOST of your marketing firepower like (ordered from most important to least):

  • Your big idea
  • Your clear goal
  • Your main qualifying pain point
  • Who you can help the most
  • A hint at your unique mechanism
  • Your risk reversal and/or guarantee

Eventually, you can get creative for this, but for now, I like having the Clear Goal as the star of the show (as your headline is the most important part of the page… and people only care about what’s in it for them).

In that case, your headline should follow this structure:

[Clear goal] Without [Big pain point or related common inconvenience of reaching that goal]

I can’t tell you exactly what to write here, so do your best and don’t aim for perfection, especially with the first draft.

3) The Subheadline

Now that we’ve used up the clear goal, and big pain point… we can formulate a compelling subheadline with your target audience, unique mechanism, and risk reversal.

4) Social Proof 1 (optional)

This step is for if you have compelling and high authority social proof to display upfront.

A lot of people use endorsements from their colleagues or from being featured in something like Forbes, Entrepreneur, or even a previous company you’ve worked with.

You can also place customer testimonials that accurately reflect the clear promise or overcoming the big problem in this area.

5) Lead

You will include another headline here to help transition on the landing page. I usually use some rendition of “ Who Will Benefit The Most From This? ” for this headline.

After a sentence that helps with transitioning into the problem, I like to go the “bullet spray” method where you list off 5-6 qualifying pain points and how they impact their life .

A list of pain points gives people multiple reasons to continue reading to find out what the solution could be (your unique mechanism).

Even if they only resonate with one of them, they may keep reading, or buy instantly.

And people just can’t help but read lists.

6) Transformation & Mechanism

Again, we are going to transition with another headline. This should be composed of the most compelling aspect of the story, something like "How We Get Results," or the name of your mechanism if it is compelling enough.

The body is where you can include a short and impactful story of your personal life, a client success, or a relevant statistic or anecdote that you can find online.

  • What was your perspective or mindset before?
  • What was the turning point?
  • How did you discover your mechanism?
  • What results did the mechanism get?
  • What IS the mechanism?

This can be treated as the “free value” aspect of the landing page where you flex your authority by giving a unique step-by-step method of how the transformation from the story above occurred.

Your job is to give away just enough to make them want more. This could come in the form of the course curriculum itself, or just a step-by-step list with an optional graphic to accompany it.

8) Offer Introduction

Yet another headline to separate this section. “Introducing: Your Course Name” tends to work well here.

This is where all of your benefits come into play.

I personally like to list out each section of my product, give it a compelling name (and focus on the outcome), and write why they should care about it.

Feature, then benefit of that feature.

If you don't know what to write here, go back and look at your qualifying pain points. Try to write benefits that solve those pain points.

Under your offer introduction is where you include more information like how the program is structured and what bonuses you give.

9) Social Proof 2 (optional)

After a headline like “Here’s What Our Customers Are Saying” this is where you will include screenshots of the social proof that you will be gathering.

We will talk about how to get testimonials in "The Social Proof" module.

In the meantime, understand that if you are just starting out you need to make it your full time job to get social proof.

But, if your product is good enough, and you have a growing audience that trusts you, you don't really need it.

10) Risk Reversal

Pop another headline here like "Our Guarantee" or something more clever if you can think of it.

If your risk reversal is complex (like guaranteeing the clear goal or else you work with them until they reach it) you need to set clear guidelines, boundaries, and disqualifiers to make it clear how they qualify for that guarantee. This lets you promise almost anything reasonable without prior results because the risk is nothing.

If you don’t have a complex risk reversal, at bare minimum promise a 100% money back guarantee if you are a beginner to build your reputation. People with larger audiences have a bit more flexibility.

11) Call To Action

The best call to actions give the reader two options.

They can either purchase your solution or continue to figure it out on their own.

The difficult part is saying this in a way that doesn’t come off bad or high pressure.

In short, remind them of a few of their pain points, then remind them of where they could be with your solution.

12) FAQ

This should be self explanatory, but include any frequently asked questions that you believe will need to be answered here. This can pair as objection handling or a place to answer questions that weren’t answered in the body copy itself.

13) Going Long Form

I am not an expert copywriter and you don’t have to be if you have an audience and talk about your expertise often.

If you decide to go long form, this is where you can have multiple sections of personal stories, client stories, or handle objections that you believe need to be given more clarity.

Writing & Editing The Landing Page

Writing your first sales page is HARD.

Don't expect it to be easy, even if you have all of the pieces right here.

Your first will be hard, your second will be easier, then you'll be able to write them without even thinking about it. That's just how skill acquisition works.

If I were to give you some tips for writing, this would be them:

  • Keep your sentences as to-the-point as possible. If your sentences seem longer than 2 lines, consider breaking them into separate sentences and keeping them punchy.
  • Always remember the transformation. If you don't know what to write, refer back to the transformation.
  • Write to one person. Have your ideal customer or the past version of yourself in mind while writing. What would they resonate with?
  • If you don't know how to start a new section, start with a problem, big idea, statistic, or quote and work from there. You can follow it with an example, description, or action steps.
  • Write your first draft without judgment, then edit it while looking at your marketing strategy template. Fill in the blanks accordingly.
  • Read your draft as if you were the ideal customer. Ask questions as you go through it and try to poke holes in your own logic. Then, fix those holes with a better argument and persuasion.
  • Make your copy as readable as possible. This isn't a book or blog post. Keep the reader going down the page.

Last but not least, don't overthink it.

Nobody can tell you how well the landing page will do until you promote it.

You will be improving this as you update your product and create new products. Marketing is about testing, not having the first iteration perfect.
