Time-Based Scarcity & Loss Aversion

a marketing original

Today, we will talk about two marketing concepts and their relation.

Let’s go over the basics first.

Scarcity

Humans value scarcity .

There are two kinds of scarcity:

  • quantity-based scarcity
    • Things with limited supply. Some examples: gold, Bitcoin, real estate, NFTs, cars, collectibles, etc.

  • time-based scarcity
    • This one is more underrated. Examples: sale promotions, raffles, concert/event tickets, time-based trials

Loss Aversion

Humans value losses more than wins.

In other words, they would do more to prevent a loss than to gain a win.

It is irrational.

People are more scared to quit their job and start a business, than to stay in the same place for 10 years. The latter is a guaranteed ticket to a predictable & miserable life of mediocrity.

Yet. The masses don’t care:

  • emotional pain from loss
  • scarcity mindset

mean they stand more to lose than what they perceive there is to gain.

You should personally avoid this, but this post isn’t about you.

This bias can be leveraged when marketing and selling products.

Tying them together

We quickly presented the two concepts - scarcity & loss aversion .

Now, we will go over:

  1. How do these two tie together?
  1. How can you use them to boost sales/conversions?
    1. Specific examples to ignite your creativity

How Do They Tie Together

Time-based scarcity comes from loss aversion.

The only reason you want to get a thing that expires in 24 hours is because you lose it after.

You can use this to your advantage.

  1. give your customer a “freebie”
  1. take it away from them in 24 hours

How To Use Them

Worth noting - these aren’t tricks that work on everything. The assumption is that you’re delivering something valuable already. Then, these strategies can be used to boost conversions/actions by a few percentage points.

Loss Aversion

When well used, the loss aversion bias can help you increase your retention .

Always remind your customer the value that you’re bringing them.

Examples:

  • Zapier shows you how many tasks have been automated
  • Brave (browser) shows you how many minutes & bandwidth they’ve saved you from blocking ads/trackers.

Fictional examples:

  • DocuSign could show the amount of hours they’ve saved you by not having to sign/send documents physically
  • Slack could show you the amount of hours saved in not having to send e-mails back and forth

The real strength comes when you create an emotional attachment to your product.

Emotional loss aversion is many times stronger than any other.

Counter Example

Loss aversion may work against you too.

People are usually less inclined to spend when they are spending physical cash . Again due to a bunch of psychological reasons.

Further, loss aversion can subtly apply to anything:

  • bad reputation? fear of a bad experience
  • negative experience? fear of the same
  • complicated/shady pricing? fear of a scam
  • etc.

Good marketing is a lot about eliminating fear in the customer.

Excellent marketing is about inspiring positive emotion in the customer.

They go hand in hand.

Time Based Scarcity

Now that we covered loss aversion, let’s see how it’s related to time-based scarcity.

Time-based scarcity can be used to both close and generate a lead .

Some obvious examples:

  • “enroll in this course in the next 7 days”
  • “engage in this Twitter raffle by RT, like, comment within 24 hours)
  • “24 hours left until the price doubles”
  • “your price is locked for the next 1 hour”

Here are some more interesting examples though:

Duolingo

The language learning app lets you keep a streak of lessons you’ve done for N days in a row. It then prominently displays it across the app.

  • indirectly shows the value they’ve given you (days of lessons, amount learned)
  • turns it into a status symbol (you see others in the app with 1000 day streaks)
  • gives you something “tangible” (a streak) that you have to continue using the app for to not lose .

Snapchat has a similar streak for messages sent between users.

These strategies keep having people come back.

It’s literally the loss aversion principle.

Beehiiv

The Substack alternative has a paid plan, but they give you a 14-day free trial when you sign up. They then immediately start bombarding you with e-mails every day until the trial ends.

This is a sort of “e-mail course” on Beehiiv, where they teach you everything about the platform. But also a loss aversion reminder.

Buy the subscription to not lose the features!

Bumble

Another real life example is the dating app Bumble. They had the problem of ghosting - two people match but one doesn’t respond.

So. They added time-based scarcity - if you don’t reply within 24 hours, the match is gone.

This allegedly helped them with a:

  • 9% increase in first messages
  • 18% increase in responses to first messages

More interestingly, they then upsell you .

Buy their premium subscription so you don’t have this pain.

Artificial Scarcity

Notice something?

These products intentionally create the (bearable) pain and then they sell you the solution to it.

This is something dominating businesses (read: business that give tons of value) can afford to do at a micro-scale.

Proton Mail allows you to create only one inbox rule on the free tier. There is no technical reason they would do this. It’s artificial scarcity.

You create the scarcity, and provide the solution.

Beehiiv doesn’t need to give you a free trial and then remind you every day of how it’s going away. You don’t even need their premium features in the first days of a newsletter.

Time Pressure

You know how when a stock is at an all time high, everybody is talking about it?

People buy in at the all time high price because they fear it’ll go higher without them having bought (FOMO).

It’s time pressure tied with loss aversion. This sort of time pressure is simply time-based scarcity with no set end date (you don’t know how long you’ve got).

What really makes the FOMO kick in, though, is seeing the thing go higher.

Seeing your time left decrease faster than you expected .

You can do the same to your products. Sell your product at X price, then every 10 sales say the price is increasing. “If you don’t act quickly, you’ll end up paying more”.

This rewards your first customers (most loyal & engaged) and encourages others to up their loyalty & engagement due to loss aversion.
