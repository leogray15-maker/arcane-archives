How To Use Humor (And 10X The Impact Of Your Writing)

The other day I watched a YouTube video that broke down the joke writing process. This brought to light some of my "unconscious competence" when it comes to writing tweets (or other content).

I wanted to share it with you all — as this will serve as a primer for a content writing live session that we will have soon.

This process has birthed some of my best Tweets.

The only difference between a good joke and impactful content is the degree of seriousness.

Before we start:

I have a feeling that many people starting out on Twitter (or any other platform) thinks that writing short-form content is quick and easy. It CAN be, but not at first.

When I was at 300 followers or so I would lay in my bed at midnight staring at my screen trying to think of a tweet idea. I would refine it to perfection and would not go to sleep unless I had content scheduled.

If you eliminate the expectation of it being "easy" at first, you will build a writing habit and it will become second nature over time. You have to put in the reps and time to get better at this.

The Joke Writing Process

When comics are writing a joke, there are a few crucial steps.

Step 1) Just Start Writing.

Seriously, just start. Write down one word. One topic. Write down a series of words. Write down a sentence that pops into your head.

You need SOMETHING written in order to get ideas flowing in the direction of that word. The act of writing down ONE word/sentence gives you something to iterate on.

Once you have something written, finish the thought and write anything else that may come to mind.

  • Personal experiences
  • Feelings associated with the topic
  • Pains or benefits associated with the topic
  • Answer the question: "What's in it for me?" (the reader)
  • Brainstorm the "how" and "why" behind the topic you wrote down
  • Etc

By now, you should have a sentence or two. It may be coherent, it may not be. That's okay.

If you need help with inspiration or idea generation, I use TweetHunter (as do most of the people that write good content).

Step 2) Create A Setup

In content, this is known as the hook. In a story, this is the setting — or giving context to what you are going to say.

Standup comedians need to set up the context before the punchline, or else the joke will flop.

Storytellers need to paint a picture of the scene, or else you won't be able to follow the story. There will be a lack of depth.

  • How can you frame the topic in an interesting way?
  • How can you grab people's attention and spark curiosity for what's next?
  • What is a problem / experience that people go through related to the topic?
  • What is the big idea that you are trying to get across?
  • Do you have a story / experience that you can set the scene for?

Again, you can use something like TweetHunter for good hook inspiration, or you can scroll through Twitter, YouTube, Medium, or Instagram (EVERYTHING you see is a hook, some good some bad).

Step 3) Get Specific

This is where true virality, laughs, and relatable stories happen — by getting specific.

  • What are the steps to achieving/relieving the benefit/pain?
  • Can you use a metaphor to help explain your point? Here is a viral tweet that uses a metaphor.
  • Can you use numbers or percentages? This helps capture attention
  • From your personal experiences — can you describe emotions, feelings, or actions with depth?

Specificity is all about relating to the reader and making them understand what you are trying to put across.

Here's what is going through the reader's mind as they are scrolling:

  • "Oh wow, this is so me"
  • "Damn, that hits deep"
  • "I believe the same thing, thanks for the confirmation"
  • "I agree with this" (think of the like and RT button as "I agree")

They aren't saying that exactly, but those are helpful for guaging the potential impact of your idea.

Another thing you can think about: would YOU like, retweet, and click on the user's profile if you saw the tweet on your timeline.

Step 4) Use Your Favorite Framework / Template

This part is optional, but effective. There are dozens if not hundreds of ways to structure a story, sales copy, or anything else that grabs and holds attention.

  • AIDA (attention, interest, desire, action)
  • Headline, lead, body, conclusion
  • Listicles ("the top 7 habits of 2024")
  • One liner tweets
  • Anything you can find in TweetHunters dashboard or resources

Many people START with this part, but it becomes 10x easier when you already have the idea fleshed out.

Writing A Tweet With This Process

Start:

The most effective thing you can do

Then (inspired by a previous tweet of mine):

The most effective thing you can do is block off 1 hour a day dedicated to the person you want to become

Idea that sparked after writing that: think of it as going on a date with yourself

Revised:

The most powerful thing you can do is set aside 1 hour a day for you and the person you want to become

Create a setup and get a bit more specific:

Set aside 1 hour a day for the 2 most important people in your life.1) You2) The person you want to become

Specificity is there, punchline is there (but could be stronger):

Set aside 1 hour a day for the 2 most important people in your life:No distractions, no phone, just you and the person you want to become.

Boom. There is a solid, specific setup and a semi relatable punchline. It has numbers, I don't give everything away upfront, and it makes people stop and think for a bit. It's also very easy to apply the advice.
