Day 2

The DNCT Rule

Copywriting is not about writing. At least, not about the usual definition of writing.

At school you've learned that writing 'well' means using complex sentences and fancy words that impress your reader.

With Copywriting, it's quite the opposite. To us Copywriters, writing well means writing clearly, and conversationally, using simple words and simple syntax...

So your reader understands you.

So it's not about being grammatically correct. Or using fancy words. Rather, it's about being as close as possible to reality.

Calling a cat a cat, and not a specimen of the feline species. This is the first rule of Copywriting.

The one you shall never forget. I call it the DNCT rule. DNCT means "Do Not Confuse Them." And you need to keep it on a sticky note somewhere on your desk, because this is the first lesson, and where most beginner Copywriters fail.

So starting now, when you write something read it again and ask yourself:

  • Does my reader understand every word I'm writing?
  • Do they have to think to get what I'm saying, or are they getting it instantly?
  • Is my writing concrete and easy to read?
  • Is my writing emotionally compelling?
  • Are my transitions logical?

If you're not certain, here are two ways to make your writing clearer:

1. Read it out loud. When you lose track of what you're reading or when you fumble a word... this is the signal that you need to rewrite the sentence.

2. Ask someone to read your Copy. And then to tell you what they've understood: Who's talking, who's the target audience, what's the problem, what's the solution, what's the big promise, etc.

Follow A Cadence

To keep the reader reading, your Copy must 'sing'.

This means following a rhythm. And rhythm comes from tension and release.

Using a combination of short sentences and longer ones, you will create a cadence. A groove.

80% of your sentences can be medium or short. 20% long. Spread them evenly so your writing doesn't feel repetitive.

Also, keep two similar arguments in the same paragraph. When you've built tension, release it with a punchline.

Just like this.

Sometimes you also have sentences that are hard to say in one breath. When you identify one, don't use a comma. Instead, split them into 2 separate sentences using a period, or an ellipsis '...'

When writing Copy you're allowed to skip using commas altogether, except when you're enumerating.

The idea is to preserve the flow of reading. And not be disturbed by unnecessary punctuation.

Delete, Delete, Delete

To keep your writing clear.....

Go back to every sentence and delete as many words as possible without the sentence losing its meaning.

This means deleting filler words. But also switching to the active voice whenever possible.

So a sentence like:

"I'm going to tell you about the time when I lost my car in the parking lot."

Becomes:

"Once I lost my car in a parking lot."

Save your reader time. Get to the point.

For instance, my mentors always told me to delete the 6 first lines of my sales letters.

Because rookie Copywriters never start in medias res (in the middle of the action.) They explain everything first. Which isn't necessary.

When writing your first pieces of Copy, follow this rule: Find the number of words you've written, and write the same letter with twice as less words. Without weakening the argument.

You will see that you always end up with more powerful Copy.

Keep it simple, stupid!

At the end of the day, keep things simple.

Effective Copy is not this mysterious thing that takes decades to master. At the end of the day, it's a simple idea brought forward through simple words.

Nothing less, nothing more.

Just remember one thing: It's about them, not you.
