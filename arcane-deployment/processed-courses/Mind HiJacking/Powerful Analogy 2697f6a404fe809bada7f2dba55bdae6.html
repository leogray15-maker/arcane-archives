Pollack points out that the power of analogies is through a process called structure mapping.

That is, we map knowledge from the original onto the new information and preserve the structure of relevant relationships while discarding the rest of the information.

Let me break it down for you:

When we hear an analogy, we compare our understanding of the analogy (say a football game) to our understanding of the idea (such as a team at work) and map everything that most clearly connects and discard the rest. It doesn’t matter that there are no hikes in a team at work because we do recognise there are quarterbacks (team leader), huddles (team meetings), and end zones (the goal).

Pollack also notes, “the more familiar we are with a given analogy, the easier the analogy is to grasp and the more likely it is to resonate.”

Pollack demonstrates that analogies are powerful as tools to influence.

But more than that, they can inherently shift the very focus of an argument between two parties.

Once a powerful analogy is established, the argument tends to shift on the fit of the analogy versus the validity of the data.

The idea that analogies are in essence short, powerful stories.

Like stories, analogies are engaging, easy to share, and provide a structure that helps make sense out of complex, chaotic ideas or concepts.

Pollack describes them as “compressed and spring-loaded stories.”

Here's the 6 Point Formula of The Most Powerful Analogy:

  • Uses the familiar to explain (and gain acceptance for) the unfamiliar
  • Highlights similarities and obscures differences
  • Switches from facts and details to abstract ideas and imagery
  • Telegraphs a coherent, understandable story
  • Resonates emotionally
  • Hard to dispute or argue against - it seems right.
