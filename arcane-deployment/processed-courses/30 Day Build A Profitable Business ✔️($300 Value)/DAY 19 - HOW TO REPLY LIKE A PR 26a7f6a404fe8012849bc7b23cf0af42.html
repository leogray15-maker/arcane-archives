DAY 19 - HOW TO REPLY LIKE A PR

Welcome to day 19

If you take any other social media course, the first growth strategy you will come across is "engage with big accounts."

In other words, you leave a comment on their content hoping that social media users see your comment, click your profile, and follow you. Since a big account has a lot of people viewing their content, many may go into the comment section, meaning you can get thousands of impressions on your "posts" just by commenting.

And of course, people don't know how to interpret this.

They set up ChatGPT bots to respond 500 times a day with the sh*ttiest non-human reply in the world and get nowhere (seriously... what are you thinking?! Stop.)

Or, they can't think of anything to reply with, but since they were told by their guru course creator to reply 25 times a day minimum, they comment "Great post bro" and then wonder why nobody follows them.

And the narrator says : "It's obvious why nobody followed them."

Spamming comments for the sake of follower growth isn't the strategy.

Meaningful dialogue is the strategy.

And it stems far beyond gaining a few followers.

  • People think in terms of tribes . Consumers on social media associate creators in groups and follow those groups. If you "join a tribe" by showing up enough in that little pocket of creators you want to join, people start associating you with them, and more people follow you.
  • Consumers aren't the only ones on social media. You are also building a relationship with other creators and high-level people, but those people don't respect you if you're just a comment spammer with nothing meaningful to say. Spamming comments is a great way to throw friendships, job opportunities, and future business partners out the window.
  • Creators love to help people they know. If you provide meaningful dialogue in the comments, the creators notice, and you are able to message them or form a connection over time. When you need help, have a question, are launching a product, or want content shared , all you need to do is ask, and they will probably help. Most of my growth as a beginner came from my "friends" (larger accounts) sharing my content.

Before you reply to any post for the sake of growing your audience, ask yourself, "why in the world would someone click my profile and follow me after they see my comment?"

A second thing to look out for is whether or not people are actually going into the comment section after reading the post.

Remember yesterday's lesson?

Well, if the original post doesn't spark debate or dialogue, then very few people are going to check the comment section.

Because of those, I'm not going to tell you to comment on 25 people's posts a day.

Frankly, 1 great comment can bring you hundreds of followers, if not more.

You simply need to get into the habit of looking for opportunities to leave a great reply.

You need to be able to identify what posts are worth your time to comment on.

You need to actually have something to say in your comment rather than trying to force something out.

Then, you have one of two options.

Simple Replies

This is your base reply strategy to invoke meaningful dialogue.

Because most of the time, the original post you are commenting on doesn't present a way to provide as much value as possible (which we will learn to do in the advanced reply option below).

But there is still a way to provide some value quickly.

Again, it's not just spamming, "great post!" or "I completely agree."

My favorite way to reply is to talk about my experience with the topic and give a bit of practical advice that people can implement.

In the comment I left above, there is one small trick here.

I said "maybe 1 out of 500 emails."

People will see that and wonder, "Who is this guy who's getting 500 cold emails?"

They also see a good-looking profile picture and assume that I am also writing content on similar topics to someone they already follow.

When you talk about yourself, people are curious to click on your profile.

  • People can relate
  • People want to know more about what you do
  • People appreciate a comment section that isn't filled with bots

You provide value by talking about what you've experienced on the topic you are commenting about.

Advanced Replies

If done correctly, this will help you grow faster than anyone else:

Make your comments better than most people's newsletters, courses, or books.

Write an engaging, in-depth post that gives the most comprehensive overview of the topic, like you're writing a short newsletter.

If I had to put a number on it, I would say make it about 200-400 words in length. About the length of your newsletter introduction section.

Yes, this will take time, but you will stand out like a sore thumb, in a good way.

The benefit of doing this is that you can also post that to your own profile, as your own content with a few shifts in how it's written.

Let's use the same example above with Justin Welsh's post about cold emails.

I could respond with something like:

  • The hook – My personal experience in the example in the image above.
  • The body – 3 actionable steps to send better cold emails
  • The conclusion – The benefits of doing it this way, such as getting more responses.

Not only would Justin deeply appreciate the value you added to his post, but he may even repost your comment to his followers.

Beyond that, you just earned your rightful place in Justin's head.

While this isn't guaranteed, any message you send to him – either asking a question or asking for help on growing your account – will be received on a much warmer note than the person he's never heard of before, and a on a lot warmer note than anyone posting ChatGPT replies in his comments.

I want to reiterate that the results from comments stem from consistency, rather than attempting to shortcut the process.

Form real connections with real people.

Even if you only do 1-5 comments a day, that adds up to quite a few people over the course of 6 months. I would find it hard to believe that you wouldn't receive any kind of life-changing opportunities in that timeframe.
